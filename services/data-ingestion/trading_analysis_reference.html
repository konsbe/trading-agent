Beyond the Charts
Tier 1 — Core Valuation
1
Earnings Per Share (EPS) & Earnings GrowthFA
The single most watched metric. Trailing EPS, forward EPS estimates, and year-over-year growth rate. Drives price-to-earnings multiples. Earnings surprises cause the largest single-session moves.
2
Revenue & Revenue GrowthFA
Top-line growth indicates business health and market share expansion. Revenue quality (recurring vs. one-time) matters as much as quantity. Declining revenue with improving margins is a warning sign.
3
Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E)FA
The market's valuation of each dollar of earnings. Compare to sector average, historical mean, and forward P/E. A high P/E means growth expectations are priced in — disappointment is punished hard.
4
Free Cash Flow (FCF) & FCF YieldFA
Cash generated after capex — cannot be faked like earnings. FCF funds dividends, buybacks, and debt repayment. FCF yield = FCF / market cap; above 5% is generally attractive.
5
Profit Margins (Gross, Operating, Net)FA
Gross margin shows pricing power. Operating margin shows cost efficiency. Net margin is the bottom line. Trend direction over 8–12 quarters is more valuable than a single snapshot.
Tier 2 — Balance Sheet & Structure
6
Debt-to-Equity (D/E) & Net Debt / EBITDAFA
Leverage amplifies both gains and losses. Net Debt/EBITDA above 4× is a red flag in most sectors. Rising interest rates make high-debt companies especially vulnerable.
7
Return on Equity (ROE) & Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)FA
ROIC > WACC means the company creates value. ROE above 15% sustained for 5+ years is a Buffett-style quality signal. Improves understanding of capital allocation decisions.
8
Cash & Liquidity PositionFA
Current ratio and quick ratio reveal short-term solvency. Large cash hoards allow buybacks and acquisitions; cash burn rate determines runway for growth companies.
9
Book Value & Price-to-Book (P/B)FA
Most relevant for banks, insurers, and asset-heavy industrials. P/B < 1 can signal deep value or fundamental distress. Tangible book value strips intangibles for a harder measure.
10
Dividend Yield, Payout Ratio & GrowthFA
High yield with a sustainable low payout ratio is ideal. Dividend growth history (Dividend Aristocrats) signals financial strength. Cuts are a serious negative catalyst.
Tier 3 — Valuation Multiples & Macro
11
EV/EBITDAFA
Enterprise value relative to operating earnings. Capital-structure neutral — better for M&A comparisons than P/E. Sector medians vary widely: tech trades higher than utilities.
12
PEG Ratio (P/E ÷ Growth)FA
Adjusts P/E for expected growth. PEG < 1 is classically undervalued. Sensitive to growth rate estimates which can be wildly wrong.
13
Interest Rates & Central Bank PolicyFA
The discount rate used in DCF models. Rising rates compress growth stock valuations most. Fed decisions are the single most market-moving macro event.
14
GDP Growth, Inflation (CPI/PPI) & Employment DataFA
Macro backdrop for all equities. CPI above target delays rate cuts. Strong employment sustains consumer spending. Leading indicators (PMI, ISM) predict these with a lag.
15
Sector & Industry DynamicsFA
Porter's Five Forces, competitive moat, market share trends, regulatory environment, and disruption risk. A great company in a dying industry is a bad investment.
16
Management Quality & Capital AllocationFA
Insider ownership, compensation alignment, share buyback vs. dilution history, acquisition track record, and candor in shareholder communications.
17
Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) ValuationFA
Intrinsic value from projected future cash flows discounted to present. Extremely sensitive to growth rate and discount rate assumptions. Powerful but requires disciplined input hygiene.
18
Guidance & Analyst Estimates (Consensus)FA
Management guidance sets the bar the market prices in. Earnings beats/misses vs. consensus drive sharp moves. Whisper numbers and revision trends matter more than the published estimate.
19
Share Buybacks & DilutionFA
Net buybacks boost EPS per share mechanically. Heavy dilution (SBC, convertibles, secondary offerings) destroys per-share value. Track share count trend over 5 years.
20
Currency Risk & Geographic Revenue MixFA
A strong USD hurts multinationals reporting in USD. Emerging market revenue adds political, currency, and demand risk. Revenue geographic breakdown is in the 10-K segment reporting.

Tier 1 — Primary Indicators
1
Price Action & Candlestick PatternsTrend
The raw foundation. Close relative to open and prior close reveals buyer/seller dominance. Key patterns: engulfing candles, hammers, doji, inside bars, pin bars. Context (support/resistance, trend) is everything.
2
Support & Resistance LevelsStructure
Price memory. Prior highs become resistance, prior lows become support. Round numbers, weekly/monthly pivots, and all-time highs/lows carry extra significance. The more times a level is tested without breaking, the stronger it is.
3
Trend & Trend LinesTrend
Higher highs + higher lows = uptrend. Trend line connects swing lows in an uptrend, swing highs in a downtrend. A broken trend line is a primary reversal signal. The trend on the higher timeframe dominates.
4
VolumeVolume
Confirms price moves. A breakout on low volume is suspect; on high volume it has conviction. Volume climaxes (extremely high volume reversals) often mark turning points. Volume precedes price.
5
Moving Averages (SMA & EMA)Trend
20, 50, 100, 200-period are the most watched. Price above the 200 MA = long-term uptrend. The 50/200 crossover (Golden/Death Cross) is widely followed. EMAs react faster; SMAs give cleaner levels.
Tier 2 — Momentum & Oscillators
6
Relative Strength Index (RSI)Oscillator
14-period default. Above 70 = overbought; below 30 = oversold. RSI divergence (price makes new high but RSI doesn't) is one of the most reliable reversal signals in all of technical analysis.
7
MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)Momentum
12/26/9 default. MACD line crossing signal line = entry signal. Histogram shrinking = fading momentum. Zero-line crosses confirm trend change. Best in trending markets; choppy markets produce false signals.
8
On-Balance Volume (OBV) & Volume ProfileVolume
OBV adds volume on up days, subtracts on down days — divergence from price signals smart money accumulation or distribution. Volume Profile shows where the most trading occurred at each price level (high-volume nodes = strong S/R).
9
Bollinger BandsVolatility
20-SMA ± 2 standard deviations. Price at the upper band is relatively extended; lower band oversold. Band squeeze (contraction) precedes expansion/breakout moves. The "Bollinger Bounce" is a mean-reversion strategy.
10
Fibonacci Retracements & ExtensionsStructure
Key levels: 23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, 78.6%. The 61.8% level (Golden Ratio) is the most powerful. Price often bounces or stalls here during a pullback. Extensions project price targets after a breakout.
Tier 3 — Secondary Indicators
11
Stochastic OscillatorOscillator
14,3,3 default. Compares closing price to range over N periods. %K crossing %D above 20 or below 80 gives signals. Better in ranging markets than RSI. Often used with RSI for confirmation.
12
Average True Range (ATR)Volatility
Measures volatility, not direction. Critical for position sizing (stop = 1.5–2× ATR from entry) and for assessing whether a move is significant or just noise within normal daily range.
13
Ichimoku CloudTrend
All-in-one system: Tenkan/Kijun crossover, price vs. cloud (Kumo), Chikou span. Price above a bullish cloud = strong uptrend. The cloud itself acts as dynamic support/resistance and projects forward 26 periods.
14
Chart PatternsStructure
Continuation: flags, pennants, triangles, wedges, cup-and-handle. Reversal: head & shoulders (most reliable), double/triple tops and bottoms, inverse H&S. Measure the pattern height to project the target move.
15
Moving Average Ribbons & MA CrossesTrend
Multiple MAs fanning in order (e.g. 10 > 20 > 50 > 200) = strong trend. Compression of the ribbon signals consolidation. The 50/200 Golden/Death Cross is watched by institutional systems.
16
Accumulation/Distribution (A/D) LineVolume
Weights volume by where price closes in its range. Rising A/D with falling price = bullish divergence (smart money accumulating). Created by Marc Chaikin; basis of the Chaikin Money Flow indicator.
17
ADX (Average Directional Index)Momentum
Measures trend strength, not direction. ADX > 25 = trending; < 20=ranging. Use +DI / -DI for direction. Critical for choosing the right strategy: trend-following vs. mean-reversion systems perform opposite in trending vs. choppy markets.
18
Pivot Points (Classic, Camarilla, Woodie's)Structure
Calculated from prior session's H/L/Close. Give PP, R1-R3, S1-S3. Intraday traders use these as daily roadmaps. The classic pivot is heavily used by market makers and floor traders.
19
Williams %ROscillator
Inverse of Stochastic. Range 0 to -100. Below -80 = oversold; above -20 = overbought. Fast-moving, works well on daily and weekly charts for spotting extreme readings. Developed by Larry Williams.
20
VWAP (Volume-Weighted Average Price)Volume
The institutional benchmark for the day. Price above VWAP = bullish intraday bias. Institutions use it to evaluate execution quality. Resets each session; heavily used in intraday and algorithmic trading.
Tier 4 — Advanced & Supplementary
21
Elliott Wave TheoryStructure
5-wave impulse + 3-wave correction. Fractal at all timeframes. Powerful when correctly identified, notoriously subjective. Best used to frame probabilistic scenarios, not as a standalone signal.
22
CCI (Commodity Channel Index)Oscillator
Measures deviation from statistical mean. ±100 thresholds for extremes. Originally for commodities but widely applied. Zero-line crossovers used as trend signals in some systems.
23
Rate of Change (ROC) / Momentum OscillatorMomentum
Simple percentage change over N periods. Zero-line crossovers signal trend change. Useful for comparing relative momentum across assets. Basis for many more sophisticated momentum strategies.
24
Parabolic SARTrend
Trailing stop-and-reverse system. Dots below price = buy; above = sell. Good for trailing stops in strong trends. In choppy markets it whipsaws badly. Often combined with ADX to filter out ranging periods.
25
Gann Angles & Time CyclesStructure
W.D. Gann's geometric angles (especially 1×1 = 45°) and time-based turning point analysis. Highly subjective and complex. Used by a small group of specialists; not mainstream but has dedicated practitioners.
26
Money Flow Index (MFI)Volume
RSI incorporating volume — "volume-weighted RSI." Above 80 = overbought; below 20 = oversold. Divergences are highly meaningful because they incorporate both price and volume pressure.
27
Market Structure (Higher Highs/Lows, Break of Structure)Structure
The foundation of Smart Money Concepts. A Break of Structure (BOS) signals trend continuation; a Change of Character (CHoCH) signals reversal. Identifies order blocks, fair value gaps, and liquidity pools.
28
Open Interest (Futures & Options)Volume
Number of open contracts. Rising price + rising OI = strong trend. Rising price + falling OI = weak rally, short covering. Options OI at strikes reveals key dealer hedging levels (gamma walls, max pain).
29
Relative Strength vs. BenchmarkMomentum
Price of asset / price of benchmark (S&P 500, sector ETF). Rising relative strength = outperforming; declining = lagging. The best stocks in bull markets consistently lead their sector on this measure.
30
Timeframe Analysis (Multi-Timeframe Confluence)Structure
Higher timeframe sets the trend direction; lower timeframe provides the entry trigger. A buy signal on the daily that aligns with a weekly uptrend and a monthly support level carries far more weight than an isolated signal.

All measure overbought/oversold conditions and momentum velocity. RSI and Stochastic are highly correlated; using both is redundant. MACD is trend-sensitive while Stochastic is range-sensitive — together they cover both regimes. MFI adds volume, giving signals extra conviction.

▼

All measure what the market is paying relative to some financial metric. They move together — a compressed P/E generally means compressed EV/EBITDA. DCF is the theoretical anchor; multiples are the market's shortcut. A stock screaming cheap on all of these simultaneously is a strong value signal.

These compound together — a company with strong gross margins tends to have strong operating margins and generates high ROIC. ROIC is the master metric that summarizes all of them. Deteriorating gross margins are the first warning sign; net margin collapse often follows.

Risk & Position Management
★
Position Sizing & Risk per TradeRisk
The single most important non-analytical skill. Risk 1–2% of capital per trade maximum. Kelly Criterion gives the theoretically optimal fraction but most traders use a fraction of full Kelly. Oversizing is the primary cause of blowups in technically correct traders.
★
Stop Loss Discipline & Risk-Reward RatioRisk
Pre-define the exit before entry. Aim for minimum 1:2 risk-reward (risking $1 to make $2). At 1:2 R/R, you only need to be right 34% of the time to be profitable. Moving stops against the trade is the most common fatal error.
★
Portfolio Correlation & DiversificationRisk
Holding 10 tech stocks is not diversification — they all crash together. Measure pairwise correlations. Diversification across uncorrelated assets (equities, bonds, commodities, real assets) reduces drawdown without proportionally reducing returns.
★
Drawdown Management & Maximum Loss ThresholdsRisk
Losing 50% requires a 100% gain to recover. Protect capital above all. A -20% drawdown rule (stop trading, reassess) can save an account. Track max drawdown, drawdown duration, and recovery time as performance metrics.
Market Microstructure & Mechanics
·
Market Microstructure (Order Flow & Bid-Ask)Micro
Level 2 quotes, order book depth, time & sales tape, bid-ask spread, slippage. Large buy orders absorbed at a level without a price move = strong support. Market makers, dark pools, and HFTs structure price formation.
·
Options Market Dynamics (Greeks, IV, Gamma Exposure)Micro
Delta, Gamma, Theta, Vega govern option pricing. Dealer gamma exposure (GEX) predicts whether market makers will amplify or dampen moves. Large OI at strikes creates "gravitational pull" toward max pain expiration.
·
Liquidity & Market ImpactMicro
A position too large for the market's daily volume cannot be exited without moving the price against you. Average daily volume, float, and short float determine liquidity. Illiquid markets amplify both gains and losses.
·
Short Interest & Short Squeeze MechanicsMicro
High short float (>20%) + rising price forces short covering, creating explosive moves. Days-to-cover = short interest / average daily volume. GameStop (2021) is the extreme case study. Short interest is reported twice monthly.
Psychology & Behavioral Finance
·
Trading Psychology (Biases & Emotional Control)Psychology
Loss aversion, confirmation bias, recency bias, overconfidence, anchoring, and FOMO are the enemy. Documented in 50 years of behavioral finance research. A trading journal is the primary tool for identifying your personal blind spots.
·
Market Sentiment & Crowd PsychologyPsychology
Greed-Fear Cycle, the Wyckoff accumulation/distribution model, Investor Sentiment surveys (AAII, CNN Fear & Greed Index). Extreme pessimism at bottoms, extreme optimism at tops — be contrarian at the extremes.
·
Process Over Outcome ThinkingPsychology
A good process can produce a bad outcome in any single trade (variance). Judging trades by outcome rather than process quality leads to random reinforcement and poor learning. Review the quality of the decision, not just the P&L.
Information & Edge
·
News, Earnings Calendars & Economic CalendarsInfo
Fed announcements (FOMC), CPI/PPI release dates, NFP, earnings dates, dividend ex-dates, stock splits. Trading through these events unhedged is speculation. Implied move (options pricing) quantifies what the market expects.
·
Institutional Positioning (COT Reports, 13F Filings)Info
Commitments of Traders (COT) from CFTC shows commercial vs. speculative positioning in futures. 13F filings (quarterly) reveal hedge fund and mutual fund equity holdings. Following what institutions are doing, not saying.
·
Insider Transactions & Corporate ActionsInfo
SEC Form 4 filings show insider buying/selling. Cluster buying by multiple insiders is strongly bullish. Corporate actions: mergers, acquisitions, spin-offs, stock splits, rights offerings — each has characteristic price behavior patterns.
·
Seasonality & Calendar EffectsInfo
"Sell in May," the January effect, holiday rallies, quarterly options expiration (OpEx), end-of-month rebalancing. Not deterministic but statistically significant over large samples. Useful as a tie-breaker when other signals are mixed.
·
Backtesting & Statistical EdgeProcess
Every strategy should be backtested over multiple market regimes (bull, bear, sideways). Key metrics: expectancy, win rate, average win/loss, max drawdown, Sharpe ratio, Sortino ratio, profit factor. Beware of curve-fitting and look-ahead bias.
·
Tax Efficiency & Cost ManagementProcess
Short-term vs. long-term capital gains treatment, tax-loss harvesting, wash-sale rules. Commissions, spread, slippage, borrowing costs (for shorts) — transaction costs are strategy-killers at high frequency. Net return after all costs is the only real number.
