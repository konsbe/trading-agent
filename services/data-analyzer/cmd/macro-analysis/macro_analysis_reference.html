Tier 1 — Central banks & interest rates
01
Central Bank Policy Rate Monetary8×/year
Fed Funds Rate, ECB Rate, BoE Bank Rate, BoJ Policy Rate — the master variable of all asset markets
▼
The single most important macro variable for all asset classes. Rate decisions set the cost of money globally. Rate hikes compress equity valuations (especially growth stocks), strengthen the currency, slow housing, and tighten credit. Rate cuts do the reverse. The pace of change and forward guidance matter as much as the level. The Fed leads global monetary conditions — when the Fed hikes, most central banks must follow or face currency weakness and imported inflation.
Strategy impact by rate regime
Cutting cycle: bullish equities, especially growth/tech. Buy duration.
Neutral/pause: range-bound, stock-picking matters more.
Hiking cycle: value over growth, banks outperform, reduce duration.
Primary data sources
federalreserve.gov (FOMC statements) FRED API — FEDFUNDS series ECB: ecb.europa.eu BoE: bankofengland.co.uk BoJ: boj.or.jp CME FedWatch Tool (rate probabilities)
What to automate
Scrape FOMC statements + minutes. LLM scores hawkish/dovish language on a −5 to +5 scale. Track CME FedWatch implied rate probabilities via CME API — this is the market's real-time rate forecast. Alert when implied probability of a hike/cut in next meeting crosses 60%.
02
Yield Curve (2s10s, 3m10y Spread) MonetaryDaily
The most reliable recession predictor in modern economics — every US recession since 1970 was preceded by inversion
▼
The spread between long-term and short-term Treasury yields. Normal (positive) yield curve = banks profitable, credit flows freely, economy expanding. Inverted (negative) = short rates exceed long rates, signaling market expects Fed will cut rates due to future weakness. The 3-month/10-year spread is the most statistically reliable recession predictor. Inversion leads recession by 12–18 months. Re-steepening AFTER inversion (as short rates fall) is the actual recession arrival signal — not inversion itself.
Historical recession signal values
2s10s >100bps: healthy, expansionary environment
2s10s 0–100bps: flattening, late cycle caution
2s10s <0 (inverted): recession warning, 12–18 month lag
Re-steepening from inversion: recession likely arriving NOW
Data sources
FRED API: T10Y2Y series (2s10s) FRED API: T10Y3M series (3m10y) Treasury.gov daily yield curve Bloomberg (if available)
03
Real Interest Rates (Nominal minus Inflation) MonetaryMonthly
The true cost of money — the most important driver of gold, growth stocks, and capital allocation
▼
Real Rate = Nominal Rate − Inflation (or use TIPS yield as direct measure). Negative real rates punish cash holding and drive capital into risk assets, gold, and real estate — the 2020–2021 asset bubble was directly caused by deeply negative real rates. Rapidly rising real rates (2022) are the most destabilizing macro event for asset prices, especially high-duration growth stocks. The 10Y TIPS yield is the cleanest real-time measure of real rates.
Real rate regimes
Real rates deeply negative (<−2%): max risk-on, buy growth/gold
Real rates −1% to +1%: balanced, normal equity environment
Real rates >2%: significant headwind for growth stocks & gold
Data sources
FRED API: DFII10 (10Y TIPS yield = real rate) FRED API: T10YIE (10Y breakeven inflation) Treasury.gov TIPS rates
04
Quantitative Easing / Tightening (QE/QT) & Fed Balance Sheet MonetaryWeekly
Central bank asset purchases that directly expand or contract global liquidity
▼
QE injects liquidity by buying bonds — expands money supply, suppresses yields, inflates risk assets. QT removes liquidity by not reinvesting maturing bonds. The Fed balance sheet expanded from $900B (2008) to $9T (2022) before QT began. Rate of balance sheet change matters as much as level. Global liquidity (sum of major central bank balance sheets) is one of the strongest predictors of risk asset performance.
Data sources
FRED API: WALCL (Fed total assets, weekly) federalreserve.gov H.4.1 release ECB: ecb.europa.eu/press/pr/wfs
What to track
Week-over-week change in Fed total assets. Net QT pace (currently ~$60–95B/month). Alert if pace changes materially — suggests policy shift before formal announcement.
05
Credit Spreads (HY & IG vs. Treasuries) CreditDaily
The "smart money" signal — credit stress appears here before equity prices reflect it
▼
Credit spread = yield of corporate bond minus equivalent Treasury yield. Widening spreads = rising perceived credit risk / risk-off. The High Yield (junk) spread is most sensitive. Credit market participants are more sophisticated than equity markets — stress in credit consistently precedes equity drawdowns by 4–8 weeks. The 2007 subprime spread blow-up preceded the equity crash by 6+ months. TED Spread (Libor minus T-bill) measures interbank stress.
Historical spread levels
HY spread <300bps: low risk, benign credit environment
HY spread 300–500bps: elevated caution, late cycle
HY spread >600bps: recession/crisis pricing. 2020: 1100bps peak. 2009: 1900bps peak.
Data sources
FRED API: BAMLH0A0HYM2 (HY spread) FRED API: BAMLC0A0CM (IG spread) FRED API: TEDRATE (TED spread) ICE BofA via FRED
Tier 2 — Bond market signals
06
10-Year Treasury Yield MonetaryDaily
The global risk-free rate — the most-watched single number after the Fed Funds rate
▼
The benchmark for all asset valuations globally. Rising 10Y compresses equity P/E multiples (especially tech/growth), raises mortgage rates, strengthens USD, and pressures EM assets. Every 100bps rise in the 10Y historically reduces equity fair value by approximately 10–15% via discount rate effect. Equity earnings yield (1/P/E) should be compared to 10Y yield to assess relative value.
Data sources
FRED API: DGS10 (10Y Treasury yield) FRED API: DGS2, DGS30 (2Y, 30Y) Treasury.gov daily rates
Equity earnings yield comparison
Earnings Yield = 1/P/E × 100. If S&P P/E = 20, EY = 5%. If 10Y = 5%, equities offer no risk premium over "risk-free" bonds. This was the 2022–2023 headwind for equities. EY − 10Y = Equity Risk Premium. Below 1% = equities expensive relative to bonds.
07
Breakeven Inflation Rate (TIPS Spread) InflationDaily
The bond market's own inflation forecast — forward-looking signal for rate trajectory
▼
5Y and 10Y breakeven inflation rates = nominal yield minus TIPS yield. Represents what bond markets expect inflation to average over the period. When breakevens rise sharply, the market expects the Fed to hike — a leading signal for rate-sensitive assets. 5Y5Y forward inflation swap (5-year inflation expectation starting in 5 years) is the Fed's preferred long-run anchoring measure.
Data sources
FRED API: T5YIE (5Y breakeven) FRED API: T10YIE (10Y breakeven) Bloomberg (5Y5Y forward)
Historical context
Breakeven stable 2.0–2.5%: anchored, Fed comfortable
Rising above 2.5%: inflation becoming unanchored risk
Above 3%: Fed will act aggressively — 2022 scenario
08
Money Supply (M2 Growth Rate) MonetaryMonthly
Broad money creation — leads inflation by 12–24 months via the monetarist transmission lag
▼
M2 includes cash, checking deposits, and near-money. Rapid M2 growth precedes inflation by 12–24 months. M2 contracted in 2022–2023 for the first time since the 1930s — a historically deflationary signal that took time to filter through. The M2 growth rate is one of Milton Friedman's core monetarist leading indicators. Watch year-over-year change rate, not absolute level.
Data sources
FRED API: M2SL (M2 money stock, monthly) FRED API: M2V (M2 velocity) Federal Reserve H.6 release
Historical ranges
M2 YoY growth 4–8%: normal, stable inflation environment
M2 growth >15%: inflationary surge approaching (2020: +27%)
M2 YoY negative: deflationary pressure 12–18M ahead
Tier 1 — Leading indicators (predict what's coming)
01
PMI — Manufacturing & Services (ISM, S&P Global) Leading1st biz day/month
The single best real-time leading indicator of economic momentum — above 50 = expansion
▼
Purchasing Managers' Index surveys procurement managers on orders, output, employment, inventories, and prices. Above 50 = expansion; below 50 = contraction. The New Orders sub-component leads the headline by 1–2 months. Released the first business day of every month — one of the most market-moving scheduled data points. Global PMIs from China (Caixin), Eurozone, UK, and Japan provide cross-market signals for commodity and EM trades.
PMI threshold signals
PMI >55: strong expansion, cyclicals outperform
PMI 50–55: moderate growth, broad market positive
PMI 45–50: slowing, shift toward defensives
PMI <45: contraction, recession risk elevated
PMI <40: severe contraction (COVID 2020: ISM hit 41.5)
Data sources
ismworld.org (ISM Manufacturing + Services) S&P Global PMI (spglobal.com) Caixin PMI: caixin.com/finance Markit EU PMI: markiteconomics.com FRED API: MANEMP, ISM series
02
Conference Board LEI (Leading Economic Index) LeadingMonthly
Composite of 10 leading indicators — three consecutive declines historically signal recession within 12 months
▼
Combines 10 forward-looking indicators: average weekly hours (manufacturing), jobless claims, new orders, ISM new orders, building permits, S&P 500, M2, interest rate spread, credit index, consumer expectations. Three consecutive monthly declines have preceded every U.S. recession since 1960. The "rule of three" — the most reliable single recession timing tool. When LEI starts declining, reduce equity exposure and increase cash/defensives.
Data sources
Conference Board: conference-board.org/topics/us-leading-indicators FRED API: USSLIND (LEI)
Signal rule
Track 6-month annualized rate of change. Below −3% with broad weakness = high recession probability. Watch both level and rate of change — level below 100 + accelerating decline is most bearish combination.
03
Initial Jobless Claims (Weekly) LeadingEvery Thursday
The highest-frequency labor market signal — released 48 hours after the reference week
▼
New unemployment insurance claims filed each week. The 4-week moving average smooths volatility. Sustained rise above 300K (from a ~200K baseline) historically precedes unemployment spikes. Any single-week print above 500K is emergency territory (COVID March 2020: 6.9M). Markets react immediately to Thursday 8:30am releases. Continuing claims (total people receiving benefits) are a coincident indicator of labor market health.
Historical thresholds
Claims <225K: tight labor market, strong economy
Claims 225–300K: normalizing, late cycle
Claims >300K (sustained): labor market breaking down
Claims >500K: crisis/severe recession
Data sources
FRED API: ICSA (initial claims) FRED API: CCSA (continuing claims) DOL.gov weekly release (Thursday 8:30am ET)
04
Housing Starts & Building Permits LeadingMonthly
Housing leads the economic cycle by 6–12 months — the first sector to feel rate changes
▼
Housing is the most rate-sensitive major sector — mortgages immediately reprice when rates change. Permits lead starts by 1–2 months. Housing accounts for 15–18% of GDP including related services (appliances, furniture, construction employment). Existing Home Sales reflect affordability conditions. The Case-Shiller Home Price Index tracks real estate asset values. When housing turns, the broader economy typically follows within 6–12 months.
Data sources
FRED API: HOUST (housing starts) FRED API: PERMIT (building permits) Census Bureau: census.gov/construction NAR: realtor.org (existing home sales) FRED API: CSUSHPISA (Case-Shiller)
Historical levels
Starts >1.5M annualized: strong housing sector
Starts 1.0–1.5M: moderate, normal range
Starts <800K: severe contraction (2009 low: 478K)
Tier 2 — Coincident indicators (confirm what's happening now)
05
GDP Growth Rate (Quarterly) Coincident3 releases/quarter
The broadest measure of economic output — two negative quarters = technical recession
▼
GDP = Consumption + Investment + Government + Net Exports. The advance estimate (released ~1 month after quarter-end) moves markets most. Revised and final estimates refine the picture. GDPNow (Atlanta Fed) provides real-time GDP tracking. GDP components reveal where growth is concentrated or collapsing — consumption declining is the most serious signal as it's 70% of GDP. A "soft landing" = GDP stays positive while inflation cools; a "hard landing" = GDP turns negative.
GDP growth regimes
GDP >3% annualized: strong expansion, cyclicals lead
GDP 1–3%: moderate growth, mixed market environment
GDP 0–1%: stall speed, recession risk rises
GDP negative ×2: technical recession (2020 Q1/Q2: −5%, −31.4%)
Data sources
BEA.gov (official GDP releases) FRED API: GDPC1 (real GDP) Atlanta Fed GDPNow (real-time): atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow NY Fed Nowcast
06
Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP) & Unemployment Rate Coincident1st Friday/month
The most market-moving monthly scheduled event — average hourly earnings is the key inflation sub-component
▼
NFP measures net new jobs added in the non-agricultural sector. Released first Friday of each month at 8:30am ET. The unemployment rate, participation rate, and Average Hourly Earnings (wage growth) are equally important. The Sahm Rule: when the 3-month average unemployment rate rises 0.5pp above its 12-month low, a recession has historically already begun. Wage growth above 4% in a 2% inflation target regime puts upward pressure on services CPI.
Historical thresholds
NFP >200K/month: strong labor market, Fed can stay hawkish
NFP 75–200K: moderate, neutral for policy
NFP <0 (job losses): recession signal, Fed will cut
Unemployment <4%: very tight labor market
Sahm Rule triggers >0.5pp rise: recession already underway
Data sources
BLS.gov (official NFP release 8:30am ET Fri) FRED API: PAYEMS (total nonfarm payrolls) FRED API: UNRATE (unemployment rate) FRED API: CES0500000003 (avg hourly earnings) ADP National Employment Report (preview)
07
Retail Sales (Monthly) CoincidentMid-month
Real-time consumer spending pulse — 70% of US GDP in monthly form
▼
Measures consumer spending at point of sale. The "control group" (ex-autos, gas, food services, building materials) is the cleanest GDP input. Month-over-month changes are volatile — use 3-month trend. Strong retail sales with falling inflation = real consumption growth (bullish). Strong retail sales with high inflation = consumers losing purchasing power (stagflation signal). Declining retail sales = consumption cracking.
Data sources
Census Bureau: census.gov/retail (monthly advance retail) FRED API: RSAFS (retail & food services) FRED API: RRSFS (real retail sales)
Signal interpretation
Real retail sales >3% YoY: healthy consumer spending
0–3% YoY: slowing, monitor closely
Negative YoY: consumer recession underway
Tier 3 — Lagging & sentiment indicators
08
Consumer Confidence (CB) & Sentiment (Michigan) LeadingMonthly
Forward-looking spending intentions — extreme pessimism is a contrarian buy signal
▼
Consumer sentiment surveys capture spending intentions before they show up in hard data. Michigan Survey: overall index + present conditions + expectations. Conference Board: similar structure. The expectations component is more forward-looking. Sharp drops in confidence (even before spending falls) are actionable for consumer discretionary sector weighting. Extremely low readings (Michigan below 60) have historically coincided with market bottoms.
Data sources
University of Michigan: sca.isr.umich.edu FRED API: UMCSENT (Michigan sentiment) Conference Board: conference-board.org FRED API: CSCICP03USM665S (CB confidence)
Contrarian signals
Michigan <60: historically near market bottoms (2022 low: 50)
Michigan 60–85: normal range
Michigan >100 + VIX <12: complacency, correction risk
09
Durable Goods Orders & Core CapEx LeadingMonthly
Business investment intentions — the leading indicator for industrial sector earnings
▼
Durable goods orders (products lasting 3+ years) signal business investment confidence. "Core capex" (non-defense, ex-aircraft) is the cleanest forward indicator of business spending. Leads industrial production by 3–6 months. Weak core capex signals business caution — often precedes earnings weakness in industrials, materials, and manufacturing sectors. Strong and accelerating capex = businesses confident in future demand.
Data sources
Census Bureau: census.gov/manufacturing/m3 FRED API: DGORDER (durable goods orders) FRED API: NEWORDER (capital goods nondefense ex-aircraft)
Signal
Core capex orders trending up >3M: business expansion mode
Core capex declining 3+ months: industrials earnings warning
Tier 1 — Core inflation measures
01
CPI — Consumer Price Index (Headline & Core) InflationMonthly, 2nd week
The primary inflation benchmark — typically the most market-moving monthly data release
▼
CPI measures price changes for a basket of consumer goods. Core CPI excludes food and energy (more volatile) — this is what the Fed targets. Shelter (Owner's Equivalent Rent) = 35% of CPI and has an 18-month lag to actual market rents, making it the longest-lasting inflation driver in rate cycles. Surprise to the upside on CPI day causes immediate bond selloff and equity rotation to defensives. Surprise to the downside = risk-on rally in growth stocks.
Historical CPI thresholds
CPI 1.5–2.5%: Goldilocks — Fed comfortable, equities thrive
CPI 2.5–4%: above target, Fed tightening bias
CPI >5%: aggressive hiking cycle (2022: CPI peaked at 9.1%)
CPI <0 (deflation): recession/demand collapse signal
Data sources
BLS.gov CPI release (2nd Tuesday of month, 8:30am ET) FRED API: CPIAUCSL (headline CPI) FRED API: CPILFESL (core CPI ex food & energy) FRED API: CUSR0000SAH1 (shelter CPI component)
02
PCE Deflator (Personal Consumption Expenditures) InflationMonthly
The Fed's preferred inflation measure — broader than CPI, with substitution adjustment
▼
The Federal Reserve explicitly targets 2% PCE (not CPI). PCE typically runs 0.3–0.5pp below CPI due to chain-weighting (adjusts when consumers substitute cheaper alternatives). Core PCE (ex food and energy) is the key metric in Fed communications. When Powell says "inflation," he means PCE. Core PCE persistently above 2.5% means the Fed will not cut rates regardless of other economic data.
Data sources
BEA.gov (PCE release — last business day of month) FRED API: PCEPI (PCE price index) FRED API: PCEPILFE (core PCE)
Fed target context
Core PCE 1.8–2.2%: at target, policy neutral
Core PCE 2.2–3%: above target, hawkish bias
Core PCE >3%: aggressive tightening mode
03
PPI — Producer Price Index (Pipeline Inflation) InflationMonthly
Wholesale price changes — leads CPI by 3–6 months as costs pass through to consumers
▼
PPI measures prices at the producer/wholesale level before goods reach consumers. Falling PPI with still-elevated CPI = disinflation in the pipeline. Rising PPI with stable CPI = margins being squeezed at the corporate level (watch for earnings misses from manufacturers). Final demand PPI is most watched; intermediate demand PPI gives earlier signal of what's coming downstream.
Data sources
BLS.gov PPI release (day after CPI, 8:30am ET) FRED API: PPIACO (all commodities PPI) FRED API: PPIFID (final demand PPI)
Lead relationship
PPI typically leads CPI by 3–6 months. When PPI is falling rapidly (as in H2 2022), expect CPI to follow down. When PPI spikes (Ukraine war Feb 2022: PPI hit +11.2% YoY), CPI surge is 3–6 months away. Monitor the PPI-CPI spread for corporate margin pressure signals.
04
Oil & Energy Prices (WTI, Brent, Natural Gas) CommodityDaily
The most important commodity in global trade — direct CPI impact and geopolitical barometer
▼
Oil is globally priced in USD. A $10/barrel oil move shifts US headline CPI by ~0.3–0.4pp. Oil also drives petrodollar recycling flows impacting EM currencies and sovereign wealth fund positioning. Energy sector earnings correlate directly with oil price. OPEC+ production decisions are political as much as economic — monitor OPEC meeting calendars. Natural gas affects European industrial competitiveness and utility sector earnings.
Macro oil price thresholds (WTI)
WTI $60–80: Goldilocks — energy affordable, sector profitable
WTI $80–100: inflationary pressure building, energy outperforms
WTI >$100: demand destruction risk, consumer headwind
WTI <$50: deflationary signal, energy sector stress
Data sources
EIA.gov (US energy data, weekly) FRED API: DCOILWTICO (WTI crude oil) FRED API: DCOILBRENTEU (Brent crude) CME Group futures data OPEC: opec.org
05
Wage Growth (Average Hourly Earnings, ECI) InflationMonthly/Quarterly
Services inflation is largely wage-driven — the stickiest component of the inflation problem
▼
Services inflation = labor costs. Wages are 60–70% of service sector costs. Employment Cost Index (ECI, quarterly) is the Fed's preferred measure — less volatile than AHE. Wage growth above 3.5% in a 2% inflation target regime creates "wage-price spiral" risk. This was the core 2022–2023 challenge: goods disinflation arrived quickly but services inflation stayed elevated because wages remained high.
Data sources
BLS.gov (AHE in NFP release) FRED API: CES0500000003 (avg hourly earnings) BLS.gov ECI (quarterly, end of Jan/Apr/Jul/Oct) FRED API: ECIALLCIV (employment cost index)
Target ranges
Wage growth 3–3.5%: consistent with 2% inflation target
3.5–4.5%: above target, services inflation sticky
>5%: wage-price spiral risk, Fed must hike more
06
Industrial Metals — Copper, Aluminum, Iron Ore CommodityDaily
"Dr. Copper" — the most reliable leading indicator of global industrial demand
▼
Copper is used in virtually every industrial process — construction, electronics, EVs, infrastructure. Its price is a real-time barometer of global industrial demand, especially China (which consumes ~55% of global copper). Iron ore tracks Chinese steel and construction activity directly. AUD/USD is called the "liquid copper proxy" — it moves faster than the underlying commodity. Falling metals = global growth deceleration; rising metals = expansion.
Data sources
LME (London Metal Exchange): lme.com Quandl/Nasdaq Data Link FRED API: PCOPPUSDM (copper price) CME Group futures
Sector impact
Rising copper + rising iron ore + rising AUD = global expansion signal. Use as a cross-asset confirmation for cyclical sector weighting. Mining stocks (BHP, RIO, FCX) lead copper by 3–6 months due to forward pricing of operations. Divergence between copper price and mining stocks = potential rotation opportunity.
Tier 1 — Currency & FX markets
01
US Dollar Index (DXY) & Dollar Strength FXDaily
The most systemic single currency — dollar strength tightens global financial conditions for everyone
▼
DXY tracks USD against EUR (57.6%), JPY (13.6%), GBP (11.9%), CAD (9.1%), SEK (4.2%), CHF (3.6%). Dollar strength: raises real debt burden of USD-denominated EM debt, suppresses commodity prices (priced in USD), hurts S&P 500 multinationals' overseas earnings, tightens global financial conditions. Every major risk-off episode features a sharp dollar surge. The DXY is both a cause and symptom of global stress.
DXY level context
DXY 95–100: neutral, broadly supportive for equities
DXY 100–105: headwind for commodities and EM
DXY >110: major global stress — EM crisis risk (2022: DXY hit 114)
Data sources
FRED API: DTWEXBGS (broad dollar index) ICE DXY index (tradingeconomics.com) Investing.com (DXY real-time) TradingView (DXY chart)
02
Yen Carry Trade & JPY Dynamics FXDaily
The world's largest carry trade — sudden yen strength triggers global deleveraging events
▼
The yen carry trade: borrow cheap in JPY (near-zero rates historically), invest in higher-yielding assets globally. When JPY suddenly strengthens (as in August 2024 when USD/JPY fell from 160 to 142 in weeks), forced carry trade unwinding cascades through EM equities, high-yield bonds, and risk assets globally. Monitor USD/JPY closely — a move below key support levels on this pair signals deleveraging risk across all risk assets simultaneously.
USD/JPY risk thresholds
USD/JPY stable or rising: carry trade intact, EM risk-on
USD/JPY rapid 5%+ decline: early carry unwind warning
USD/JPY rapid >10% decline: systemic deleveraging event
Data sources
FRED API: DEXJPUS (USD/JPY) Bank of Japan: boj.or.jp CFTC COT report (JPY positioning)

04
Trade Policy, Tariffs & Trade Wars GeopoliticalEvent-driven
Tariffs are simultaneously inflationary and recessionary — the most complex policy risk to model
▼
Tariffs raise import prices (inflationary) while reducing trade volume (recessionary). The 2018–2019 US-China trade war caused equity volatility and accelerated supply chain restructuring. The 2025 tariff escalation created significant uncertainty across industrials, consumer goods, and tech hardware. Sector impacts are targeted: steel tariffs hit auto and construction; semiconductor export controls hit tech; agricultural tariffs hurt farming communities with retaliation risk. Currency movements often partially offset tariff impacts.
Monitor
USTR.gov (US trade representative) WTO.org (trade dispute filings) Commerce.gov (trade statistics) Peterson Institute for International Economics (piie.com)
Sector exposure matrix
Steel/Aluminum tariffs → Autos, Construction, Industrials. Semiconductor export controls → Chip equipment, EDA software. Agricultural tariffs → Grains, farm equipment (DE, AGCO). Consumer goods → Retail margins, apparel. Track affected sectors in your scoring model as macro headwind.
05
Geopolitical Risk Events (Wars, Sanctions, Political Instability) GeopoliticalEvent-driven
Non-linear tail risks — when they materialize, normal correlations break down
▼
Armed conflicts disrupt energy, food, and supply chains. Russia-Ukraine (Feb 2022) triggered the largest European energy crisis in decades, caused global grain supply disruption, and accelerated NATO defense spending (bullish for defense stocks globally). Middle East tensions spike oil 5–15% within days. Taiwan Strait risk is the largest systemic geopolitical tail risk — it would affect global semiconductor supply and trigger massive capital flight from Asian markets. The GPR (Geopolitical Risk Index) quantifies risk level historically.
Monitor
GPR Index: matteoiacoviello.com/gpr (free download) Global Conflict Tracker: cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker GDELT Project: gdeltproject.org (news event database) Stratfor: stratfor.com (geopolitical intelligence)
Sector impact by event type
Middle East conflict → Energy ↑, Airlines ↓, Defense ↑. Russia/Ukraine → Energy ↑, Grains ↑, European industrials ↓. Taiwan tensions → Semiconductors ↓ globally, Defense ↑. EM political crisis → EM equities ↓, USD ↑, Gold ↑.
06
Fiscal Policy, Deficits & Debt Sustainability FiscalQuarterly/Annual
Large fiscal deficits increase Treasury supply and can push long-term yields up regardless of Fed policy
▼
The "fiscal dominance" concern: if deficits are large enough, rising bond supply pushes long yields higher even when the Fed is cutting short rates — this happened in H2 2023. US debt ceiling crises create systemic stress around potential technical default. UK Truss budget (2022) caused gilt yields to spike 150bps in days and GBP to crash — a textbook example of market discipline on fiscal excess. CBO (Congressional Budget Office) projections set long-term Treasury supply expectations.
Data sources
CBO.gov (budget outlook) Treasury.gov (monthly budget statement) FRED API: FYFSD (federal surplus or deficit) IMF Fiscal Monitor: imf.org
Risk thresholds
Deficit <3% of GDP: manageable, no supply pressure
Deficit 3–6% GDP: elevated, watch term premium
Deficit >6% GDP (peacetime): market may demand higher yields
07
Institutional Positioning — COT Reports & 13F Filings PositioningWeekly/Quarterly
What large institutional players are actually doing — not what they're saying
▼
CFTC Commitments of Traders (COT) report shows commercial (hedger) vs. speculative (trend follower) positioning in futures markets. Extreme speculative net long positions are contrarian bearish signals. 13F filings (quarterly, 45 days after quarter-end) reveal hedge fund and mutual fund equity holdings. When top-quality funds (Ackman, Tepper, Einhorn) initiate large positions, their fundamental thesis is worth examining. Aggregate 13F data shows sector rotation trends by institutional money.
Data sources
CFTC COT: cftc.gov/MarketReports/CommitmentsofTraders (weekly Friday) SEC 13F: sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar Dataroma.com (guru 13F aggregator) Whalewisdom.com (13F analytics) Barchart COT data
COT contrarian signals
When commercial hedgers (who know their industry best) are at extreme net long positions AND specs are extreme net short — strong contrarian buy. The opposite pattern is a reliable top signal. Most useful for commodities (gold, oil, grains) and currencies where hedger/speculator distinction is cleanest.

The most important macro transmission chains — how one variable causes moves in another across markets. These are the relationships your system's regime classification must understand to correctly weight each signal.

1. The primary rate transmission chain
The most important macro chain — how Fed decisions ripple through every asset class with different lags.
Cause Effect Direction Typical lag Trading implication
Fed hikes rates Short-term yields rise Direct Immediate Money market funds become attractive vs. equities
Short rates rise Yield curve flattens / inverts Direct Weeks Bank stocks underperform (compressed net interest margin)
Real rates rise Growth stocks compress Inverse Immediate to months Duration effect: DCF values fall as discount rate rises. Worst for unprofitable tech.
Rate hikes USD strengthens Direct Days to weeks Headwind for S&P 500 multinationals (20–40% international revenue)
USD strengthens Commodities fall Inverse Weeks Commodity producers (energy, materials) underperform; commodity importers benefit
Rate hikes Housing slows Inverse 3–9 months Homebuilders, REITs, mortgage lenders all underperform
Yield curve inverts Bank lending tightens Direct 3–6 months Credit contraction → small business stress → employment weakness
Credit tightens Recession risk rises Direct 6–18 months Reduce cyclical exposure; increase cash/defensives/short duration
