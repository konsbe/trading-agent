Sentiment analysis quantifies the psychological state of market participants — where they are on the fear/greed spectrum and how that positioning creates contrarian opportunities. Extreme readings in sentiment indicators are among the most reliable reversal signals in all of trading.

Tier 1 — Options market sentiment
01
Put/Call Ratio (PCR) SentimentOptions
Ratio of put options bought vs. call options — the most direct measure of hedging demand and fear
▼
The PCR = put volume / call volume. Above 1.0 means more puts than calls being bought — elevated fear or hedging. The equity-only PCR (excluding index options which are institutionally driven) is the cleanest retail sentiment signal. Extreme readings are contrarian: when everyone is buying puts (PCR very high), the market is already pricing in the fear and a bounce often follows. VIX and PCR together provide the most complete options sentiment picture.
Contrarian signal thresholds (equity-only PCR)
PCR >1.2: extreme fear — contrarian bullish signal
PCR 0.9–1.2: elevated anxiety, market near-term bottom
PCR 0.6–0.9: normal range, no clear signal
PCR <0.5: excessive complacency — contrarian bearish
Data sources
CBOE: cboe.com/data/historical-options-data Barchart.com (daily PCR, free) Market Chameleon: marketchameleon.com Polygon.io options chain API
02
Implied Volatility Skew & Term Structure SentimentOptions
How the market prices risk asymmetrically across strikes and time — the options market's true fear signal
▼
IV Skew measures the difference in implied volatility between out-of-the-money puts and calls. Normally, OTM puts trade at higher IV than OTM calls (negative skew) because institutions pay a premium for downside protection. When skew steepens sharply, fear is rising. When skew flattens unusually, complacency is extreme. Term structure shows IV across different expirations — normally upward sloping (longer = more uncertainty). An inverted term structure (near-term IV above long-term) signals acute near-term fear.
Skew interpretation
Steep negative skew (puts much more expensive than calls): extreme fear, often near bottom
Normal skew: modest put premium, healthy hedging activity
Flat/positive skew: call premium exceeds put — retail call buying frenzy, bubble signal
Data sources
CBOE SKEW Index: cboe.com Polygon.io options chain (IV by strike) Market Chameleon IV skew charts Interactive Brokers TWS (term structure)
03
Short Interest & Days-to-Cover Sentiment
How much of the float is being bet against — and how long it would take shorts to exit
▼
Short Interest = shares sold short / total float. Days-to-Cover (Short Ratio) = shares short / average daily volume. High short interest creates two conditions: (1) contrarian opportunity if the short thesis is wrong — forced covering amplifies upside (short squeeze), (2) warning signal if shorts are correct — informed money is betting against the company. Context determines which. High DTC (above 10) means a squeeze could run for many days as shorts cannot exit quickly.
Signal thresholds
Short interest >20% float + strong catalyst = squeeze setup
Days-to-cover >10: powerful squeeze potential if triggered
Short interest 5–15%: moderate, monitor for trend changes
Short interest rising rapidly (>5pp in one reporting period): institutional concern signal
Data sources
FINRA short interest (bi-monthly): finra.org/investors/learn-to-invest/advanced-investing/short-sale-statistics Shortvolume.com (daily estimates) Yahoo Finance (statistics tab) Finviz (short float %)
04
Margin Debt & Leverage Levels Sentiment
Total borrowed money used to buy stocks — peaks before major market tops, crashes before bottoms
▼
Margin debt is money borrowed from brokers to buy securities. It amplifies both gains and losses. When margin debt peaks and starts declining, the forced liquidation of leveraged positions creates cascading selling. The rate of change matters more than the absolute level — a rapid decline in margin debt signals forced deleveraging. NYSE margin debt data is published monthly with a 1-month lag. In 2000 and 2007, margin debt peaked near equity market tops.
Strategy signals
Margin debt at all-time high + PCR low + VIX low: major top risk
Margin debt declining rapidly: forced selling underway — avoid buying
Margin debt at cycle lows: leverage washed out — better risk/reward for entry
Data sources
FINRA margin statistics (monthly): finra.org/investors/learn-to-invest/advanced-investing/margin-statistics Yardeni Research: yardeni.com (charts)
05
IPO Activity & SPAC Issuance as Cycle Indicators Sentiment
Frothy issuance activity historically marks late-cycle euphoria — a cycle top indicator
▼
When IPO markets are hot and companies of questionable quality go public at extreme valuations, it signals peak investor risk appetite. The 1999–2000 dot-com peak had 547 IPOs in one year, many of money-losing companies. The 2020–2021 SPAC mania (more than 1,000 SPACs formed) was the most extreme modern example of late-cycle froth — the SPAC index lost over 70% from peak to trough. Conversely, a dried-up IPO market signals peak pessimism and often coincides with equity troughs.
Contrarian signals
IPO market red-hot, SPACs booming, average IPO pops >50% on day one: late cycle
IPO market frozen, companies withdrawing listings: pessimism peak, often near bottom
Data sources
Renaissance Capital: renaissancecapital.com/review SEC EDGAR S-1 filings monitor SPAC Research: spacresearch.com

Factor analysis is the academic underpinning of systematic investing. Five factors have produced statistically significant excess returns over decades across multiple markets and time periods. These are the building blocks of your scoring engine — your system implicitly uses all five.

The five academically validated return factors
01
Quality Factor Factor
High ROIC, strong margins, low debt, consistent earnings — the most durable factor across all regimes
▼
Quality stocks are companies with high return on equity, stable earnings growth, low debt, and strong free cash flow conversion. They tend to outperform in late-cycle and recessionary environments because their businesses are resilient. Quality is the factor most aligned with Warren Buffett's approach. It is the most regime-independent factor — it works in bull and bear markets, though it shines brightest when conditions deteriorate.
Construction metrics
High gross profitability (Novy-Marx) · High ROIC · Low accruals (earnings quality) · Low leverage · Stable earnings (low EPS volatility) · High interest coverage · Consistent FCF generation
Historical performance
Annual alpha vs. market: +2 to +4% historically
Best regime: late cycle + recession (defensive quality)
Worst regime: early bull (low-quality rallies hardest off bottom)
Data sources & ETF proxies
QUAL ETF (iShares MSCI USA Quality Factor) Simfin API (calculate ROIC, margins) Ken French data library: mba.tuck.dartmouth.edu/pages/faculty/ken.french/data_library.html
Your system link
Your QntFA scoring layer IS the quality factor. ROIC, FCF yield, margin trend, D/E — these are exactly the quality factor metrics. Ensure your composite score overweights quality in late-cycle macro regimes.
02
Momentum Factor Factor
Stocks that have outperformed recently tend to continue outperforming — the strongest factor by Sharpe ratio
▼
The momentum factor (Jegadeesh-Titman, 1993) is one of the most robust in finance — it has been documented in 40+ countries and 200+ years of data. Buy the top-performing stocks of the past 12 months (excluding the most recent month to avoid short-term reversal). Sell or avoid the bottom performers. The 12-1 momentum (12-month return minus most recent month) is the standard construction. Momentum crashes occur in sudden market reversals (March 2009, March 2020) — it is the factor with the highest Sharpe ratio but also the worst tail behavior.
Construction methodology
Rank all stocks by 12-month total return (excluding most recent month). Long top quintile. In your system: track 12-1 momentum score for each stock in universe. Weight it heavily in trending macro regimes (ADX >25 equivalent at macro level).
Historical performance
Annual alpha: +5 to +9% in trending markets
Best regime: trending bull markets, clear macro direction
Crash risk: −40% drawdowns in sudden reversals (momentum crash)
ETF proxies & data
MTUM ETF (iShares MSCI USA Momentum) Calculate from Polygon.io / yfinance price data AQR momentum data: aqr.com/insights/datasets
Momentum crash protection
When VIX spikes above 30 AND momentum factor draws down >10% in one month, momentum is in a crash regime — reduce momentum weight to near zero until VIX normalizes below 25. This single rule dramatically improves momentum factor risk-adjusted returns.
03
Value Factor Factor
Cheap stocks outperform expensive ones over the long run — but requires patience and regime awareness
▼
The value factor (Fama-French, 1992) buys stocks with low price-to-book ratios and sells expensive ones. Modern implementations use composite value scores (P/E, P/B, EV/EBITDA, P/FCF) rather than just P/B. Value had a lost decade 2010–2020 as growth dominated, then staged a powerful reversal in 2022 when rates rose. Value works best when rates are rising (discounts expensive cash flows more), economic growth is robust, and inflation is moderate to high. Requires a minimum 3–5 year time horizon to capture the premium.
Best construction for your system
Use composite value: equal weight P/E (vs sector median), EV/EBITDA (vs sector median), FCF yield, P/B. Rank universe. Top quintile = value stocks. Combine with quality filter (eliminate value traps by requiring ROIC > 8%).
Regime performance
Best: rising inflation + rising rates (2022 example)
Good: early-mid economic recovery
Worst: falling rates + low inflation (2010–2020)
ETF proxies & data
VLUE ETF (iShares MSCI USA Value Factor) IVE ETF (iShares S&P 500 Value) Ken French HML factor data library
Value trap avoidance
The biggest failure mode: buying cheap stocks that deserved to be cheap. Filter: (1) ROIC must be above 8%, (2) revenue must not be in sustained decline (>2 years), (3) debt must be manageable (Net Debt/EBITDA <4×). Cheap + deteriorating = value trap. Cheap + stable/improving = genuine value.
04
Low Volatility Factor Factor
Low-risk stocks have historically delivered higher risk-adjusted returns than high-risk stocks — the "volatility anomaly"
▼
Counter-intuitive to standard finance theory (which says higher risk = higher return). In practice, low-beta stocks have delivered better risk-adjusted returns over long periods. This "low volatility anomaly" exists because institutional investors are constrained to benchmark-relative mandates and chase high-beta for career reasons, causing low-beta stocks to be systematically underpriced. Best for capital preservation in late cycle and bear markets. Tends to lag in strong bull markets.
Construction
Rank universe by trailing 12-month realized volatility (daily returns standard deviation). Long bottom quintile (lowest volatility). Alternatively: rank by beta vs. S&P 500 — long stocks with beta <0.8. For your system: use ATR / price (normalized volatility) as a scoring metric.
ETF proxies
USMV ETF (iShares MSCI Min Volatility) SPLV ETF (Invesco S&P 500 Low Vol) Calculate beta from yfinance data
05
Size Factor (Small Cap Premium) Factor
Small caps have historically outperformed large caps over full cycles — but with much higher volatility
▼
The size factor (Fama-French SMB — Small Minus Big) documents that small capitalization stocks generate excess returns over large caps over long periods. The premium compensates for liquidity risk, information asymmetry, and higher business risk. The size premium is strongest in early economic recovery phases and in low-rate environments. For your beginner strategy (large-cap focus), this factor is noted but not actively exploited until the system is more mature.
Best regimes for small caps
Early recovery: small caps typically lead by +10–15% in first year
Falling rates: small caps (more domestic, more floating rate debt) benefit most
Rising rates + strong USD: small caps underperform (higher debt costs)

Intermarket analysis studies how bonds, equities, commodities, and currencies interact. These relationships are not always stable — regime changes alter correlations fundamentally. Understanding when correlations break down is as important as knowing when they hold.

The four markets & their traditional relationships
01
Bond-Equity Correlation & Regime Shifts Intermarket
The most important inter-market relationship — and the one that changed fundamentally in 2022
▼
From 2000–2021, bonds and equities were negatively correlated (bonds rose when equities fell) — the foundation of the 60/40 portfolio. In 2022, this correlation turned sharply positive (both fell simultaneously) as inflation forced central banks to hike rates. This was the most damaging year for 60/40 since 1937. The bond-equity correlation is NEGATIVE in deflationary recessions (bonds are safe haven) and POSITIVE in inflationary recessions (bonds also sell off). Understanding which regime you're in determines whether bonds actually hedge your equity exposure.
Correlation regimes
Deflationary environment: bonds negative corr with equities (−0.5 to −0.8). 60/40 works.
Inflationary environment: bonds positive corr with equities (+0.3 to +0.6). 60/40 fails.
Transition zone (inflation falling toward target): correlation uncertain, monitor CPI trend.
Data sources
FRED API: DGS10 vs SPY — calculate 60-day rolling correlation TradingView: ratio charts and correlation tool Calculate with pandas .corr() on yfinance data
System application
Your system should track the 60-day rolling bond-equity correlation. When it turns positive (above +0.3 for 30+ days), the macro regime classification should shift to "inflationary" and reduce bond holdings as a hedge while increasing cash and real assets. This single variable has enormous portfolio construction implications.
02
Commodity-Equity Lead-Lag Relationship Intermarket
Commodities are an inflation early warning system — they typically lead equity sector rotation by months
▼
Rising commodity prices signal inflationary pressure building in the pipeline, typically 3–6 months before it appears in CPI. This gives your system advance warning to rotate from growth into value/energy/materials. The CRB (Commodity Research Bureau) index is the broadest commodity benchmark. Copper specifically leads global industrial equity performance. Gold vs. copper ratio (gold rising relative to copper) signals fear/deflation; copper rising relative to gold signals confidence/inflation.
Key intermarket ratios
Gold/Copper ratio rising → deflationary fear, buy bonds/gold, sell industrials

Gold/Copper ratio falling → inflationary growth, buy cyclicals/commodities, reduce gold

CRB index rising >10% YoY → commodity inflation entering pipeline, watch CPI in 3–6M
Data sources
FRED: PCOPPUSDM (copper) and GOLDAMGBD228NLBM (gold) Refinitiv CRB Index via TradingView Calculate ratio in Python from FRED data
03
Currency-Equity Intermarket Relationships Intermarket
Specific currency pairs are proxies for risk appetite, commodity demand, and EM health
▼
Certain currency pairs act as leading indicators for specific equity sectors and markets. AUD/USD tracks China demand and commodity equities. USD/JPY tracks the global carry trade and risk appetite. EUR/USD reflects Fed-ECB policy divergence. The correlation between these pairs and equity sectors provides additional confirmation signals that are independent of both fundamental and technical analysis.
Currency-equity lead relationships
AUD/USD rising → Copper miners, iron ore, materials stocks bullish (China demand proxy)

USD/JPY rising → Carry trade intact, risk-on, EM equities supported

USD/JPY falling rapidly → Carry unwind, global risk-off, reduce all risk assets

USD weakening broadly → EM equities, gold, commodities all bullish simultaneously
Data sources
FRED: DEXJPUS, DEXUSAL, DEXUSEU Polygon.io forex API TradingView forex charts

Flow analysis and market microstructure study how actual orders move markets — the mechanics beneath price. As options markets now represent a force that can drive underlying equity prices on a daily basis, understanding flow is no longer optional for serious systematic traders.

01
Unusual Options Activity (UOA) Flow
Large, unusual options orders that suggest informed money is positioning for a specific move
▼
UOA occurs when options volume massively exceeds open interest in a specific strike, or when a large single-ticket order is placed in a normally quiet expiration. These are often (not always) informed trades — insiders, fund managers, or speculators with a strong thesis. The key filters: (1) volume > 10× open interest at that strike, (2) order is a market buy (urgency), (3) out of the money, (4) short-dated expiration. When multiple unusual options prints stack up in the same direction on the same stock in a short window, the signal strengthens considerably.
Quality filters for UOA
Volume >10× OI + market buy + OTM + 30-60 DTE: highest conviction
Sweep order across multiple exchanges simultaneously: institutional urgency
Large block but on ask: could be hedge, not directional
Volume only slightly above OI: background noise, ignore
Data sources
Unusual Whales: unusualwhales.com (free tier) Market Chameleon: marketchameleon.com Barchart.com unusual options activity Polygon.io options API (build custom scanner) Tradytics.com (options flow)
02
Gamma Exposure (GEX) & Dealer Hedging Flow
How options market maker hedging mechanically amplifies or dampens equity price moves
▼
When options dealers sell calls, they buy the underlying stock to hedge (positive gamma = they buy rallies and sell dips = dampens volatility). When dealers are short gamma (sold puts), they must sell the underlying as it falls — amplifying downtrends. Gamma exposure maps tell you whether the market is in a "pinned" regime (high positive gamma, low vol, price stays near large OI strikes) or a "unstable" regime (negative gamma, explosive moves likely). This mechanistic force has grown dramatically as options volumes have surged — on SPX, options now drive equity prices more than the reverse.
GEX regimes
High positive GEX: market "pinned" near major strikes, low vol expected, sell vol
GEX near zero: neutral, no mechanical vol suppression or amplification
Negative GEX: dealer short gamma, moves are amplified — expect explosive vol
Data sources
SqueezeMetrics (free GEX data): squeezemetrics.com SpotGamma: spotgamma.com Unusual Whales GEX tracker Calculate from Polygon.io options chain (complex)
03
Dark Pool Prints & Block Trades Flow
Institutional-size trades executed off-exchange — where the real money moves
▼
Dark pools are private exchanges where institutional investors trade large blocks without revealing their orders to the public market before execution. Dark pool volume as a % of total volume above 40–50% signals heavy institutional activity. A large dark pool print at a specific price level that subsequently becomes support signals institutional accumulation at that price. Dark pool data is reported with a delay and requires careful interpretation — they can be buys or sells and are often used to reduce market impact of large orders over days or weeks.
Data sources
FINRA dark pool data: otctransparency.finra.org Unusual Whales dark pool tab Shortvolume.com (dark pool % of volume) Tradytics dark pool tracker
Interpretation rules
Dark pool print at a price that becomes repeated support = institutional buyer at that level. Combine with OBV rising: if OBV rising while dark pool volume is elevated, institutions are accumulating. Dark pool % of volume rising while price falls = sellers being absorbed — potential bottom formation.
04
Options Max Pain & OpEx Effects Flow
The price at which the maximum number of options expire worthless — gravitational pull near expiration
▼
Max Pain is the strike price at which the combined value of all in-the-money options (calls and puts) is minimized — theoretically the price at which option sellers (typically market makers) pay out the least. Some argue this creates a gravitational pull as expiration approaches. Monthly options expiration (third Friday) and quarterly OPEX create systematic price patterns. The week before OPEX is often volatile; OPEX week itself often sees price pinning near major strikes. This effect is most pronounced in individual stocks with high options OI concentration.
OpEx calendar effects
Week before monthly OpEx: elevated vol, directional moves
OpEx Friday: price often pins near max pain level into close
Week after OpEx: often directional — new positioning being established
Data sources
Maximum Pain calculator: maxpain.com Market Chameleon (max pain by stock) Calculate from Polygon.io options OI data

Seasonality patterns are statistically significant tendencies that persist over decades. They are not deterministic — they are probabilistic tilts that, combined with other signals, improve the odds. Never trade seasonality alone, but always check it as a tie-breaker or confirmation layer.

02
Presidential Election Cycle Seasonality
4-year US election cycle creates statistically significant patterns in equity markets
▼
Year 2 (midterm year) of a presidential term is historically the weakest for equities, often producing corrections as policy uncertainty peaks and the president's party typically loses congressional seats. Year 3 (pre-election) is historically the strongest — the incumbent administration stimulates the economy to improve re-election prospects. Year 4 (election year) is typically positive but volatile around the election itself. Since 1950, the S&P 500 has never had a negative return in year 3 of the presidential cycle.
Historical cycle averages (S&P 500)
Year 1 (post-election): +6.7% avg — policy honeymoon
Year 2 (midterm): +5.4% avg but volatile, often corrects first
Year 3 (pre-election): +16.4% avg — strongest year historically
Year 4 (election): +6.6% avg — positive but choppy pre-election
Data sources
Stock Trader's Almanac (annual publication) Yardeni Research: yardeni.com Calculate from yfinance historical S&P returns
03
Options Expiration (OpEx) & Quarter-End Flows Seasonality
Systematic mechanical flows around key calendar dates create repeatable price patterns
▼
Monthly OpEx (3rd Friday): price pinning near max pain levels. Week before: elevated vol. Week after: often directional as new positioning establishes. Quarterly OpEx (March, June, September, December — "triple witching"): largest of all, involves equity options, index options, AND futures expiring simultaneously — creates the highest volatility of any regular calendar event. Quarter-end (last 3 days): large fund rebalancing — window dressing boosts recent winners, sells laggards. Month-end: pension fund rebalancing creates systematic equity/bond flows predictable from prior month performance.
Key calendar dates to automate
Monthly OpEx: 3rd Friday of each month. Triple witching (quarterly OpEx): 3rd Friday of Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec. Month-end: last 2 trading days. Quarter-end: last 3 trading days. FOMC blackout period: 10 days before each FOMC meeting — Fed governors cannot speak publicly, reducing one source of volatility.
Data sources
CBOE calendar: cboe.com/trading-resources/calendars Fed FOMC calendar: federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomccalendars.htm Investing.com economic calendar (all events)

Alternative data provides information about business conditions before it appears in official financial statements. Hedge funds have used these signals for years — many are now accessible to sophisticated retail and systematic traders. These inputs feed your qualitative scoring layer.

01
Web Traffic & App Download Data Alt Data
Real-time business activity signals before quarterly earnings — user engagement is a leading revenue indicator
▼
Web traffic to a company's website and app download rankings provide real-time signals of user engagement and growth trends weeks before quarterly earnings. A sudden spike in web traffic to an e-commerce site before earnings likely signals strong revenue. Declining app downloads for a subscription company signal churn risk. Sensor Tower and SimilarWeb are the primary data providers. These signals had major alpha before becoming widely known — they are now somewhat crowded but still useful for directional confirmation.
Data sources
SimilarWeb: similarweb.com (free estimates) Sensor Tower: sensortower.com (app data) Google Trends: trends.google.com (free, search interest) Semrush: semrush.com (web traffic analytics) App Annie: data.ai
Application in system
For consumer-facing companies in your universe: track 3-month trend in SimilarWeb visits and Sensor Tower app ranking before earnings. Rising +10%+ = bullish signal for upcoming quarter. Feed to LLM layer for structured output: {"web_traffic_trend": "accelerating", "signal": "bullish", "confidence": 0.7}.
02
Job Posting Data Alt Data
Corporate hiring intentions are forward-looking — job postings predict revenue growth 1–2 quarters ahead
▼
Companies hire ahead of expected growth and freeze hiring before downturns — making job postings a leading indicator of future revenue and capex. LinkedIn job postings, Indeed trends, and Lightcast data provide this signal. A tech company suddenly posting 200% more ML engineer roles is signaling an AI investment ramp before it appears in financial results. Mass layoff announcements (WARN Act filings) signal the opposite — cost-cutting before a revenue miss.
Data sources
LinkedIn job trends (observable manually) Indeed hiring trends: indeed.com/hiring-lab JOLTS (Job Openings and Labor Turnover Survey): FRED JTSJOL Lightcast: lightcast.io (paid, institutional) WARN Act filings: dol.gov/agencies/eta/layoffs
Signal interpretation
Job postings up >30% YoY: growth acceleration signal
Mass layoff WARN filing: cost-cutting, earnings risk
Hiring freeze (flat postings): cautious mgmt, uncertainty
03
Satellite & Shipping Data Alt Data
Physical world activity measured from space and shipping databases — the original alternative data
▼
Satellite imagery of retail parking lots (counting cars = foot traffic = revenue proxy), oil storage tanks (measuring shadow levels = supply signal), shipping vessel tracking (cargo volumes = trade activity), and agricultural field analysis (crop yield estimates) were among the first alternative data sources used by hedge funds. Container shipping rates (Shanghai Containerized Freight Index) are a real-time supply chain and inflation signal freely available online.
Free accessible proxies
Shanghai Containerized Freight Index (SCFI): en.sse.net.cn Vessel tracking: marinetraffic.com (free) EIA crude oil storage: eia.gov (weekly) Baltic Dry Index: FRED DBDI series
Institutional data providers
SpaceKnow: spaceknow.com Orbital Insight: orbitalinsight.com RS Metrics: rsmetrics.com
04
Social Media & Search Trend Sentiment Alt Data
Real-time public sentiment that can drive retail-dominated stocks and flag emerging trends
▼
Reddit (WallStreetBets), Twitter/X, StockTwits, and Google Trends data measure retail sentiment and emerging narrative momentum. Google Trends for a company's product name rising sharply = consumer interest increasing before it shows in revenue. WallStreetBets mention count surging = retail attention, potential squeeze conditions. Social sentiment is most predictive for small-to-mid cap consumer stocks where retail investor participation is high. For large caps and macro, it is mostly noise.
Data sources
Google Trends: trends.google.com (free API) Reddit API: reddit.com/dev/api StockTwits API: stocktwits.com/developers Twitter/X API (elevated access required) Swaggy Stocks: swaggy.stocks.com (WSB analysis)
Signal quality note
Social sentiment is the noisiest alt data signal. Use it as a 5% weight or less in your composite. It is most useful as an extreme contrarian indicator: when retail sentiment (WSB, StockTwits) is 90%+ bullish on a stock, it often marks a short-term top. When 90%+ bearish, a short-term bottom. Do not follow the crowd — fade the extremes.

Event-driven analysis identifies specific corporate or market events that create time-limited, repeatable trading opportunities independent of broader market conditions. These are the clearest examples of an identifiable edge with a defined catalyst and timeline.

01
Merger Arbitrage (Risk Arb) Event-Driven
Capturing the spread between current acquisition target price and deal price — a near risk-free return with tail risk
▼
When a company announces it will acquire another at $X per share, the target typically trades at a discount to $X (the "spread") — reflecting deal failure risk. Merger arb = buy the target at market price, capturing the spread when the deal closes. Return = spread / time to close. The risk is deal failure (regulatory block, financing failure, target rejection) — which typically causes the target to fall 20–40% back to pre-deal levels. The strategy requires careful assessment of deal certainty: strategic logic, regulatory environment, financing terms, and competing bidder probability.
Return expectations
Cash deals (certain): spread 1–3%, annualized 6–15% if closes in 3–6 months
Stock-for-stock: more complex, need to hedge acquirer stock
Regulatory risk (antitrust): spread widens sharply if risk rises
Data sources

Spin-offs & Corporate Restructurings Event-Driven
Spin-offs historically outperform the market by 10–15% in the first 2 years — a documented anomaly
▼
When a company spins off a division, the new entity is often sold by passive investors who received the shares without wanting them (index funds rebalancing), creating temporary undervaluation. Joel Greenblatt's "You Can Be a Stock Market Genius" documented this effect extensively. The parent company also often outperforms after a spin-off as management focuses on the core business. Spin-offs with high insider ownership and operating in niche markets with strong growth are the best candidates.
What to look for
High insider ownership at spinco: management confidence
Parent wanted to divest (not showcase) spun unit: potential hidden gem
Spinco in faster-growing industry than parent: focus premium
Parent loading spinco with debt before separation: avoid

03
Index Inclusion & Rebalancing Event-Driven
Predictable forced buying when stocks are added to major indices — front-run the mandatory rebalance
▼
When a stock is added to the S&P 500, all S&P 500 index funds must buy it — creating predictable demand. S&P 500 additions are announced ~3 trading days before the actual inclusion date. Historically, stocks gain 3–7% on the announcement and continue rising into inclusion. After inclusion, the "index addition premium" often reverses. Russell index reconstitution (annual in June) is the largest forced rebalancing event — small/mid cap stocks near the cutoff experience extreme volatility around reconstitution.
Strategy approach
Buy S&P 500 addition announcement: capture 3–5 day run-up
Sell on or before actual inclusion date: premium reversal begins
Russell "add" candidates identified in May: buy before June rebalance

04
Earnings Season Patterns & Pre-Announcement Drift Event-Driven
Stocks with positive estimate revision trends drift upward before earnings — a systematic tradeable pattern
▼
Post-Earnings Announcement Drift (PEAD): stocks that beat earnings estimates continue to drift in the direction of the surprise for weeks afterward — one of the most documented market anomalies. Pre-earnings drift: stocks with rising EPS revisions tend to trend upward in the 30–60 days before their earnings date. "Buy the rumor, sell the news" applies most in highly anticipated events where price has already moved significantly into the announcement. The implied move (derived from options IV) defines the expected reaction range.
Key signals
Rising EPS revisions + rising RS line 30 days pre-earnings: bullish setup
Beat + raise guidance: PEAD trade — continue holding, drift continues
Beat estimates but guide down: often sells off despite beat
Large move into earnings: "sell the news" — reduce before announcement

Earnings Whispers: earningswhispers.com Yahoo Finance earnings calendar Seeking Alpha earnings analysis Estimate revision data: Finviz, Zacks

Relative value analysis identifies mispricings between related securities — it is market-neutral in theory, capturing the spread between two instruments rather than a directional bet on the market. It is the foundation of many hedge fund strategies.

01
Pairs Trading (Statistical Arbitrage) Rel. Value
Long one correlated stock, short another — profit from the spread mean-reverting
▼
Two stocks in the same sector that historically move together will occasionally diverge. The strategy: when the spread (ratio or price difference) moves 2+ standard deviations from its historical mean, go long the underperformer and short the outperformer — betting the spread will revert. Classic pairs: Coke/Pepsi, Goldman/Morgan Stanley, ExxonMobil/Chevron. The key risk: pairs can stop being correlated when a fundamental divergence occurs (one company's business changes structurally). The system must distinguish temporary vs. permanent divergences.
Entry signal
Spread >2 StdDev from 1-year mean: enter the trade
Cointegration confirmed (ADF test p <0.05): pair is mean-reverting
Spread 1–2 StdDev: watch, not yet ready to enter
Fundamental divergence (earnings growth diverging): abandon pair
Implementation tools
Python: statsmodels (cointegration test) yfinance for price data Quantopian Zipline / QuantConnect for backtesting pandas .corr() for rolling correlation monitoring
Python pseudo-code structure
# Step 1: Test cointegration
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import coint
score, pvalue, _ = coint(stock_a, stock_b)
# If pvalue < 0.05: pair is cointegrated (mean-reverting)

Sector vs. Sector Relative Value Rel. Value
Identifying which sectors are cheap relative to each other on a normalized basis — the rotation framework
▼
Rather than comparing individual stocks, compare entire sectors. Technology vs. Financials P/E spread, Energy vs. Utilities EV/EBITDA spread, Growth vs. Value relative performance. When a sector trades at historically wide discount to its typical relative valuation, the mean reversion trade is to overweight the cheap sector and underweight the expensive one. This is the core of your rotation strategy — expressed as cross-sector relative value.
Key cross-sector ratios to track
XLK/XLV (Tech vs. Healthcare): growth vs. defensive
XLE/XLU (Energy vs. Utilities): inflation sensitivity
XLF/XLK (Financials vs. Tech): value vs. growth
IWM/SPY (Small vs. Large): risk appetite and cycle position
GLD/SPY (Gold vs. Equities): risk-off ratio

03
Capital Structure Arbitrage Rel. Value
Trading the same company's equity vs. debt when they are mispriced relative to each other
▼
When a company's credit risk (reflected in bond yields/CDS spreads) diverges sharply from what equity implied volatility suggests, a relative value opportunity exists. Example: equity options imply low vol (market complacent) but CDS spreads are widening (credit market concerned) — go long the CDS (buy protection) and sell equity vol (sell puts). This is an advanced institutional strategy but the signal it provides — watching for divergence between equity and credit markets for the same company — is accessible to any trader through public data.
Accessible version for your system
When stock price is near 52-week high (equity market unconcerned) but: (1) the company's bonds are trading at wide spreads, or (2) short interest is rising rapidly, or (3) credit rating agencies have issued downgrades — this is a capital structure divergence signal. Reduce equity exposure or implement a hedge.
