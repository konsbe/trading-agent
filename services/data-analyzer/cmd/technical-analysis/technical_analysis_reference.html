Tier 1 — Foundation layer
01
Price Action & Candlestick Patterns Structure
The raw foundation — all other indicators are derivatives of OHLC price data
▼
Price action is the study of raw OHLC (Open, High, Low, Close) data. Every other indicator is a mathematical derivative of price. The relationship between open and close, and how price moves within a candle's range, reveals buyer/seller dominance in real time. Context is everything — the same candle pattern at a major support level carries far more weight than the same pattern in the middle of a range.
Key bullish patterns
Bullish engulfing · Hammer · Morning star · Piercing line · Bullish harami · Pin bar (long lower wick) · Inside bar breakout upward
Key bearish patterns
Bearish engulfing · Shooting star · Evening star · Dark cloud cover · Bearish harami · Doji at resistance (indecision near highs)
Strategy ranges
Pattern at key S/R + volume confirmation = high probability
Pattern in open space, no S/R context = low weight
Pattern against higher-timeframe trend = avoid
Data sources
TradingView (free) yfinance Python lib Polygon.io API Alpaca Markets API IBKR TWS API
02
Support & Resistance Levels Structure
Price memory — where supply and demand historically concentrated
▼
Support is a price level where buying interest historically exceeds selling pressure, causing price to bounce. Resistance is the inverse. Prior highs become resistance; prior lows become support. Once a resistance level breaks and holds, it becomes new support (role reversal). Round numbers, 52-week highs/lows, all-time highs, and weekly/monthly pivots carry extra psychological significance. The more times a level is tested without breaking, the stronger it appears — but also the more likely it will eventually fail as sellers are exhausted.
Types of S/R
Horizontal levels · Trend line touches · Moving average confluences · Round numbers (psychological) · Prior ATH/ATL · Weekly/monthly open · VWAP · Volume Profile nodes
Strength scoring
3+ touches + volume spike at level: very strong
2 touches, moderate volume: moderate
1 touch, low volume: weak, treat as tentative
Data sources
TradingView (manual markup) Polygon.io historical OHLCV TA-Lib Python (peak detection)
Best timeframes
Weekly Daily 4H 1H
03
Trend Structure (HH/HL & LH/LL) Trend
The market's directional bias — the single most important context for all other signals
▼
An uptrend is defined by a series of Higher Highs (HH) and Higher Lows (HL). A downtrend by Lower Highs (LH) and Lower Lows (LL). A ranging market produces roughly equal highs and lows. All indicators should be interpreted through the lens of the prevailing trend — a bullish RSI divergence in a strong downtrend is a weak signal; the same divergence at the end of a pullback in an uptrend is a strong signal. Always identify the higher-timeframe trend before analyzing lower timeframe signals.
Trend classification
Uptrend: HH + HL pattern intact, price above 50 MA
Ranging: price oscillating between defined S/R
Downtrend: LH + LL pattern, price below 50 MA
Trend line rules
Uptrend line: connect 2+ swing lows. Needs 3rd touch to be valid. Break of trend line = caution. Retest of broken trend line = confirmation. Trend lines on weekly charts outweigh daily.
04
Chart Patterns Structure
Recurring price formations with statistically measurable breakout behavior
▼
Chart patterns represent the visual footprint of crowd psychology playing out repeatedly. Continuation patterns occur mid-trend; reversal patterns signal trend change. The measured move (pattern height projected from breakout) gives a price target. Volume on the breakout candle is the primary confirmation signal — a breakout on low volume has a much higher failure rate.
Reversal patterns
Head & Shoulders (most reliable) · Inverse H&S · Double Top / Bottom · Triple Top / Bottom · Rising/Falling Wedge (at trend end)
Continuation patterns
Bull/Bear Flag · Pennant · Ascending / Descending Triangle · Symmetrical Triangle · Cup & Handle · Rectangle consolidation
Measurement rule
Target = Breakout level ± Pattern height. H&S target = Neckline − (Head − Neckline). Always check R/R: target must be ≥2× your stop distance.
Data sources & tools
TradingView pattern recognition TA-Lib Python (pattern functions) Polygon.io OHLCV for backtesting
05
Fibonacci Retracements & Extensions Structure
Natural proportion levels where price frequently reverses or stalls
▼
Fibonacci ratios (23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, 78.6%) are drawn from a significant swing high to swing low (or vice versa) to identify likely pullback reversal zones. The 61.8% level (the Golden Ratio) is the most powerful. Extensions (127.2%, 161.8%, 261.8%) project price targets beyond the prior swing, most useful after a breakout to a new high. Power increases significantly when Fibonacci levels coincide with prior S/R, moving averages, or volume nodes — these are "confluence zones."
Key levels & their strength
61.8% (Golden Ratio): highest probability reversal
38.2%: common shallow pullback level in strong trends
50%: psychological mid-point (not a true Fib ratio)
78.6%: deep pullback — trend still technically intact
Break of 78.6%: trend likely reversing
Best use cases
Identify entry zones on pullbacks in established uptrends. Combine with RSI oversold readings at the 61.8% level for highest-probability entries. Use extensions (161.8%) as profit targets after breakouts. Works on all timeframes — most reliable on daily and weekly charts.
Data sources
TradingView (built-in Fib tool) TA-Lib / pandas-ta Python Manual calc from Polygon OHLCV
Confluence rule
A Fibonacci level that overlaps with a prior S/R level, a major moving average, AND a volume node is a "triple confluence" zone — one of the highest-probability entry points in all of TA.
06
Pivot Points (Classic, Camarilla, Woodie's) Structure
Mathematically derived daily/weekly S&R levels used by institutional traders
▼
Calculated from the prior period's High, Low, and Close. They give the Central Pivot (PP), three resistance levels (R1, R2, R3), and three support levels (S1, S2, S3). Heavily used by market makers and floor traders — these levels are self-fulfilling because so many participants watch them. Weekly pivots are more significant than daily. Monthly pivots are major structural levels.
Formula (Classic)
PP = (H + L + C) / 3
R1 = 2×PP − L  ·  S1 = 2×PP − H
R2 = PP + (H−L)  ·  S2 = PP − (H−L)
R3 = H + 2×(PP−L)  ·  S3 = L − 2×(H−PP)
Strategy use
Price above weekly PP: bullish bias for the week
Price at R1 or S1: first target / first S/R
Price reaches R3 or S3: extreme extended move
Data sources
TradingView (auto-plots) pandas-ta Python library Calculate from Polygon.io OHLCV
Best timeframes
Monthly Weekly Daily 4H
Tier 1 — Primary trend indicators
01
Moving Averages (SMA & EMA) Trend
Smoothed price series that reveal trend direction and act as dynamic S/R
▼
Simple Moving Average (SMA) gives equal weight to all periods. Exponential Moving Average (EMA) weights recent prices more heavily, making it more responsive. The most watched levels are 20 (short-term momentum), 50 (medium-term trend), 100, and 200 (long-term trend). Price above the 200-day MA = long-term uptrend; below = long-term downtrend. The 50/200 Golden Cross (50 crosses above 200) is a major bullish signal; Death Cross is bearish. EMAs better for active trading; SMAs better for identifying major structural levels.
SMA(n) = (P1+P2+...+Pn) / n  |  EMA = P × k + EMA_prev × (1−k)  |  k = 2/(n+1)
Key levels & their meaning
20 MA: short-term momentum, used for swing trade entries
50 MA: medium-term trend filter, institutional benchmark
100 MA: intermediate support in bull markets
200 MA: the master long-term trend line
Strategy ranges
Price > 200 MA + 50 > 200 MA: strong uptrend, buy dips
Price near 200 MA: critical decision zone
Price < 200 MA + 50 < 200 MA: downtrend, sell rallies
Data sources
TA-Lib Python pandas-ta Python TradingView alerts Polygon.io + calculate
02
MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) Momentum
Trend-following momentum indicator — the most versatile single indicator in TA
▼
MACD = 12-period EMA minus 26-period EMA. The Signal Line is a 9-period EMA of MACD. The Histogram shows the difference between MACD and Signal. Works best in trending markets — generates many false signals in choppy ranges. Three signal types: (1) MACD crossing Signal line (entry/exit), (2) MACD crossing zero line (trend confirmation), (3) Divergence between MACD and price (reversal warning). The histogram shrinking is the earliest warning of fading momentum.
MACD Line = EMA(12) − EMA(26)  |  Signal = EMA(9) of MACD  |  Histogram = MACD − Signal
Signal types ranked by reliability
Bullish divergence (price lower low, MACD higher low): strongest
Zero-line cross upward: trend confirmation
Signal line cross: entry signal, confirm with price action
Histogram cross (earliest but most false signals)
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (MACD function) pandas-ta TradingView Alpaca / Polygon + compute
03
Ichimoku Cloud (Kumo) Trend
All-in-one system: trend, momentum, S/R, and signals in a single view
▼
Five components: Tenkan-sen (9-period conversion), Kijun-sen (26-period base), Senkou Span A (leading span — avg of Tenkan+Kijun plotted 26 periods ahead), Senkou Span B (52-period midpoint plotted 26 periods ahead), Chikou Span (close plotted 26 periods back). The cloud (between Spans A and B) is dynamic support/resistance projected into the future — its twist and color indicate trend direction. Price above a bullish cloud = strong uptrend. The cloud width indicates volatility and S/R strength.
Signal hierarchy
Price above cloud + cloud bullish + Tenkan > Kijun: strongest bull
TK cross above cloud (Tenkan crosses Kijun): buy signal
Price inside cloud: consolidation/indecision zone
Price below cloud + cloud bearish: strong downtrend
Data sources
TA-Lib Python pandas-ta (ichimoku) TradingView
04
ADX (Average Directional Index) Trend
Measures trend strength — not direction. The strategy selector.
▼
ADX tells you HOW STRONG the trend is, not which direction. This is critical for strategy selection: trend-following indicators work in trending markets (ADX >25) and fail in ranging markets (ADX <20). The +DI and −DI lines show direction. ADX rising + +DI > −DI = strengthening uptrend. ADX rising + −DI > +DI = strengthening downtrend. ADX falling = trend weakening. Most important use: filter out oscillator signals when ADX <20 (ranging) and filter out trend-following signals when ADX peaks and rolls over.
ADX readings & meaning
ADX >40: very strong trend, momentum strategies work
ADX 25–40: trending, use trend-following tools
ADX 20–25: weakening or building trend
ADX <20: ranging, use oscillators not trend indicators
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (ADX function) pandas-ta TradingView
05
Parabolic SAR Trend
Dynamic trailing stop system — dots below price = buy, above = sell
▼
Dots below price indicate an uptrend; dots above indicate a downtrend. A dot flip signals stop-and-reverse. Excellent for trailing stop placement in strong trends. In choppy/ranging markets it whipsaws constantly — always combine with ADX to confirm trend strength before using. Acceleration Factor (default 0.02) controls sensitivity — lower AF = fewer signals but more lag.
Strategy use
Use ONLY when ADX >25 — confirmed trending market
Place stop loss at SAR dot level, trail it as trend extends
ADX <20: SAR generates whipsaws — disable signal
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (SAR) pandas-ta TradingView
Tier 1 — Primary oscillators
01
RSI (Relative Strength Index) Oscillator
The most important momentum oscillator — measures speed and magnitude of price change
▼
RSI oscillates 0–100. Default 14-period. Above 70 = overbought (doesn't mean sell — in strong uptrends RSI can stay above 70 for weeks). Below 30 = oversold. The most powerful RSI signal is divergence: price makes a new high but RSI fails to confirm it (bearish divergence) — or price makes a new low but RSI holds higher (bullish divergence). Divergences on weekly charts are the strongest reversal signals in all of technical analysis. RSI failure swings (where RSI breaks its own prior swing) are also highly reliable.
RSI = 100 − (100 / (1 + RS))  |  RS = Average Gain / Average Loss over N periods
Signal types
Bullish divergence on weekly: highest conviction reversal
RSI <30 in uptrend: high-probability pullback entry
RSI >70 in uptrend: normal — reduce new buys, don't sell
Bearish divergence + RSI >70: exit / reduce signal
Regime-adjusted levels
Bull market: RSI oscillates 40–90. Use 40 as oversold.
Bear market: RSI oscillates 10–60. Use 60 as overbought.
Ranging: Classic 30/70 levels apply.
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (RSI) pandas-ta TradingView Alpaca + compute
02
Stochastic Oscillator Oscillator
Compares closing price to range — excels in ranging markets where RSI lags
▼
%K measures where price closes within its recent range (0–100). %D is a 3-period moving average of %K. Default settings: 14,3,3. Above 80 = overbought; below 20 = oversold. The %K/%D crossover in oversold/overbought zones generates entry signals. Stochastic divergence (price vs. %D) is a reliable reversal signal. Most effective in ranging markets — combines well with RSI where Stochastic provides the entry timing and RSI provides the momentum filter.
%K = ((Close − Lowest Low) / (Highest High − Lowest Low)) × 100  |  %D = 3-period SMA of %K
Strategy use
%K crosses %D upward from below 20: buy signal
Both lines exit oversold zone heading up: confirmation
%K crosses %D downward from above 80: sell signal
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (STOCH) pandas-ta TradingView
03
Williams %R Oscillator
Fast-moving momentum oscillator — inverse of Stochastic, great for short-term timing
▼
Range −100 to 0. Readings above −20 = overbought; below −80 = oversold. More sensitive than RSI — reacts faster to price changes. Developed by Larry Williams. Works well on daily and weekly charts for spotting extreme readings before a reversal. Often produces faster signals than RSI but with more false positives — use RSI as confirmation.
%R = ((Highest High − Close) / (Highest High − Lowest Low)) × −100
Strategy ranges
%R below −80 (oversold) turning up: buy signal
−80 to −20: neutral zone
%R above −20 (overbought) turning down: sell signal
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (WILLR) pandas-ta
04
CCI (Commodity Channel Index) Oscillator
Measures deviation from statistical mean — detects cyclical extremes
▼
CCI measures how far price has moved from its statistical average. Originally designed for commodities but applied widely to all markets. ±100 are the primary threshold levels for overbought/oversold. ±200 represents extreme readings. Zero-line crossovers can be used as trend signals. CCI turning from extreme levels (±200) is one of the stronger reversal signals. Works on all timeframes and asset classes.
CCI = (Typical Price − SMA of TP) / (0.015 × Mean Deviation)  |  TP = (H+L+C)/3
Strategy ranges
CCI below −100 turning up: oversold reversal
CCI cross above 0 from below: bullish trend signal
CCI above +200 turning down: extreme overbought
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (CCI) pandas-ta
05
Rate of Change (ROC) / Momentum Oscillator Momentum
Pure percentage price change over N periods — the basis of momentum strategies
▼
ROC = ((Current Price − Price N periods ago) / Price N periods ago) × 100. Simplest momentum measure. Zero-line crossovers = trend change. Above zero = positive momentum, below = negative. Used in cross-sectional momentum strategies (rank assets by 12-month ROC, buy top quartile, short bottom quartile). The 12-1 momentum factor (12-month return minus most recent 1 month) is one of the most robust factors in quantitative equity investing.
Strategy use
12M ROC top quartile of universe: momentum buy signal
ROC crossing above 0: trend turning positive
ROC bottom quartile: momentum avoidance/short signal
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (ROC) pandas-ta Calculate from Polygon price data
06
Relative Strength vs. Benchmark Momentum
Is this stock outperforming or underperforming its benchmark?
▼
Not to be confused with RSI. This is simply: Price of Asset / Price of Benchmark (S&P 500 or sector ETF). Rising RS line = outperforming. Falling RS line = underperforming. The best stocks in a bull market consistently show rising RS lines before their price breakouts. In your rotation strategy, only buy stocks where RS is in an uptrend relative to the S&P 500 and their sector. RS breakout often precedes price breakout by 2–4 weeks — it's a leading indicator of institutional accumulation.
Strategy rules
RS line at new 52-week high while price near highs: strongest bull
RS line flat: inline with market, neutral
RS line in downtrend: avoid even if price seems cheap
Data sources
Calculate: stock price / SPY price TradingView (ratio chart) Finviz RS Rating
Tier 1 — Volume analysis
01
Raw Volume & Volume Analysis Volume
Volume precedes price — the most important confirmation tool in all of TA
▼
Volume is the number of shares or contracts traded. It confirms or denies every price signal. A breakout above resistance on 3× average daily volume is highly reliable. The same breakout on 0.5× volume is suspect and likely to fail. Volume spikes at key levels often mark turning points — climactic buying or selling volume exhausts the dominant side. Rising price with falling volume = trend losing conviction. Falling price with low volume = weak selling, likely to bounce.
Volume confirmation rules
Breakout on >2× avg volume: valid, high conviction
Pullback on low volume in uptrend: healthy, buy the dip
Breakout on avg volume: monitor for follow-through
Breakout on <0.75× avg volume: likely false breakout
Rising price + consistently falling volume: distribution
Data sources
Polygon.io OHLCV API yfinance Python Alpaca Markets API IBKR TWS API
02
VWAP (Volume-Weighted Average Price) Volume
The institutional benchmark — the most important intraday reference price
▼
VWAP = (Cumulative Price × Volume) / Cumulative Volume. Resets each session. Institutions use VWAP to evaluate execution quality — buying below VWAP is "good execution," above is "bad." Price above VWAP = bullish intraday bias; below = bearish. VWAP acts as strong support in uptrends and resistance in downtrends. Anchored VWAP (anchored to a specific date, e.g., earnings release or major low) provides longer-term institutional reference levels.
Strategy use
Price above VWAP + rising: strong intraday uptrend
Pullback to VWAP in uptrend: institutional buy zone
Price below VWAP + declining: avoid long entries
Data sources
Polygon.io (intraday data) Alpaca Markets API Calculate from minute OHLCV data TradingView (built-in)
03
OBV (On-Balance Volume) & A/D Line Volume
Cumulative volume flow — reveals smart money accumulation before price moves
▼
OBV adds volume on up days, subtracts on down days. The A/D (Accumulation/Distribution) Line is more nuanced — it weights volume by where price closes within the day's range, not just whether it's up or down. OBV and A/D divergences from price are the most powerful volume signals: if price makes a new high but OBV fails to confirm, smart money is distributing into strength. If price makes a new low but OBV holds, institutional accumulation is occurring underneath.
OBV: if Close > Prev Close, OBV = OBV + Volume; if less, OBV = OBV − Volume  |  A/D = CLV × Volume + Prev A/D  |  CLV = ((Close−Low)−(High−Close)) / (High−Low)
Divergence signals
Price lower low + OBV higher low: bullish divergence (accumulation)
Price higher high + OBV lower high: bearish divergence (distribution)
OBV trending up persistently = institutional accumulation phase
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (OBV, AD) pandas-ta TradingView
04
Volume Profile (VPVR) Volume
Shows volume traded at each price level — reveals where institutions positioned
▼
Unlike time-based volume bars, Volume Profile shows how much volume traded at each price level over a period. High Volume Nodes (HVN) = strong S/R (price spent a lot of time here — big institutional positions). Low Volume Nodes (LVN) = price will move quickly through these (thin trading, no institutional anchor). Point of Control (POC) = the single price with highest volume — often acts as magnet. Value Area = 70% of all volume (High VAH, Low VAL = key boundaries).
Key levels
Price above VAH: breakout from distribution, bullish
Price returns to POC from above: strong support test
Price in Value Area: range-bound, look for mean reversion
Price below VAL: breakdown, bearish
Data sources
TradingView Volume Profile Polygon.io intraday + custom calc Sierra Chart NinjaTrader
05
MFI (Money Flow Index) Volume
Volume-weighted RSI — divergences carry extra conviction because they include both price and volume
▼
MFI = RSI with volume weighting. Range 0–100. Above 80 = overbought; below 20 = oversold. Because it incorporates volume, MFI divergences are considered higher conviction than pure RSI divergences. When price makes a new high but MFI falls (declining volume on higher price), it means the rally is losing institutional support — a warning to reduce exposure.
Strategy ranges
MFI <20 turning up with price bounce: strong buy
Bullish MFI divergence (price lower, MFI higher): accumulation
MFI >80 + bearish divergence: distribution warning
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (MFI) pandas-ta TradingView
06
Open Interest (Futures & Options) Volume
Number of outstanding contracts — reveals conviction behind price moves in derivatives markets
▼
OI is the total number of open derivatives contracts. Price up + OI up = new money entering, strong trend. Price up + OI down = short covering (weaker rally, longs may be taking profits). Price down + OI up = new shorts entering, strong downtrend. Price down + OI down = longs liquidating (may be nearing capitulation). Options OI at specific strikes creates "gravitational" price effects — large call/put OI at a strike causes market makers to hedge, pulling price toward that strike near expiration (max pain theory).
OI interpretation matrix
Price ↑ + OI ↑ = Strong bull
Price ↑ + OI ↓ = Weak rally
Price ↓ + OI ↑ = Strong bear
Price ↓ + OI ↓ = Capitulation near
Data sources
CFTC COT Report (cftc.gov) Polygon.io options chain Barchart.com options OI Market Chameleon
Tier 1 — Volatility indicators
01
Bollinger Bands Volatility
Price envelope based on standard deviation — squeeze precedes explosive moves
▼
Three bands: 20-period SMA (middle), ±2 standard deviations (upper/lower). About 95% of price action falls within the bands. A "Bollinger Squeeze" (bands narrowing sharply) indicates low volatility compression — a high-volatility breakout move follows. The direction of the breakout is not predicted by the squeeze itself — needs directional confirmation. Band walk: price tracking along the upper band = very strong uptrend. "W bottom" inside the lower band is a strong reversal pattern.
Middle Band = 20-period SMA  |  Upper = SMA + (2 × StdDev)  |  Lower = SMA − (2 × StdDev)
Strategy signals
Squeeze + breakout above upper band with volume: buy
Price tags lower band in uptrend: mean reversion buy zone
Price in middle: no edge, wait for band extreme
Price persistently below middle band: downtrend
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (BBANDS) pandas-ta TradingView
02
ATR (Average True Range) Volatility
The essential stop-loss and position-sizing tool — measures daily volatility in price terms
▼
ATR measures the average range of price movement over N periods (default 14), accounting for gaps. Unlike standard deviation, it's measured in price terms not percentages, making it directly usable for stop placement. Set stops at 1.5–2.5× ATR below entry — this keeps stops outside normal noise. Position size = (Account Risk %) / (ATR multiple × ATR value). This is the single most important position sizing input in technical trading.
True Range = Max(High−Low, |High−Prev Close|, |Low−Prev Close|)  |  ATR = 14-period EMA of TR
Stop placement rule
Low-volatility stock (ATR 1–2%): stop at 1.5× ATR
Normal volatility (ATR 2–4%): stop at 2× ATR
High volatility (ATR >5%): stop at 2.5× ATR, reduce size
Position sizing formula
Risk $ = Account × 1% (or 2% for high-risk)
Stop Distance = 2 × ATR × Share Price
Shares = Risk $ / Stop Distance
Data sources
TA-Lib Python (ATR) pandas-ta Polygon.io OHLCV + compute
Rising vs falling ATR
Rising ATR = volatility expanding (trend accelerating or reversal). Falling ATR = compression (consolidation before breakout). ATR at multi-month lows = Bollinger Squeeze equivalent — major move imminent.
03
VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) & IV Volatility
The "fear gauge" — market-implied expectations of future volatility
▼
VIX measures the 30-day implied volatility of S&P 500 options — it represents what options traders are paying for protection. VIX and equity prices are strongly inversely correlated. VIX spikes above 30 correspond to fear-driven selloffs and often mark short-term market bottoms (mean-reversion buy opportunity). VIX below 15 = complacency. Individual stocks have their own IV (Implied Volatility) which drives options pricing and signals expected move size around events.
VIX regime levels
VIX >35: extreme fear, often a buying opportunity
VIX 20–35: elevated anxiety, market choppy
VIX 15–20: normal, moderate hedging activity
VIX <12: complacency, correction risk rising
Data sources
CBOE VIX data (cboe.com) Yahoo Finance (^VIX symbol) FRED API (VIXCLS series) Polygon.io options chain for IV
04
Keltner Channels Volatility
ATR-based channel — used alongside Bollinger Bands for the squeeze setup
▼
Keltner Channels use ATR instead of standard deviation for the band width. When Bollinger Bands contract inside Keltner Channels — the "Bollinger Band Squeeze" setup — it signals extremely low volatility and a high-probability explosive breakout approaching. This is one of the most reliable volatility setups in technical analysis. John Carter's TTM Squeeze indicator is based on this relationship.
Middle = EMA(20)  |  Upper = EMA(20) + (2 × ATR(10))  |  Lower = EMA(20) − (2 × ATR(10))
Squeeze setup rules
BB inside Keltner + momentum turning up: buy the breakout
BB inside Keltner, direction unclear: wait, do not enter
BB outside Keltner: no squeeze, normal volatility regime
Data sources
pandas-ta (kc function) TradingView (built-in) TA-Lib ATR + EMA to build manually
Advanced — Smart Money Concepts & Market Structure
01
Smart Money Concepts (SMC) — Order Blocks & FVGs Structure
Institutional footprint analysis — where large orders were placed in the market
▼
SMC aims to identify where institutional orders were executed by analyzing market structure. Order Blocks are the last opposing candle before a strong impulse move — institutions often re-enter at these levels. Fair Value Gaps (FVG/Imbalance) are price gaps where the market moved so fast that one side was not fully filled — price tends to return to fill these. Break of Structure (BOS) confirms trend continuation. Change of Character (CHoCH) signals a potential reversal. Liquidity sweeps occur when price briefly breaks a swing point to trigger stop orders before reversing.
Key SMC concepts
Order Block (OB): Last down candle before strong up move = bullish OB. Price often returns to test it.

Fair Value Gap (FVG): 3-candle pattern where candle 1 and 3 don't overlap. Price "fills the gap" on return.

Liquidity sweep: Stop-hunt above prior high / below prior low before real move in opposite direction.
BOS vs. CHoCH
BOS (Break of Structure): Price breaks a swing high/low in the direction of trend = trend continues.

CHoCH (Change of Character): Price breaks structure in the OPPOSITE direction = potential reversal beginning. First CHoCH is a warning; second CHoCH is confirmation.
Data sources
TradingView (manual / LuxAlgo indicator) Polygon.io OHLCV + custom Python detection Custom backtest: scan for 3-candle FVG patterns
02
Elliott Wave Theory Structure
Fractal market structure — 5-wave impulse + 3-wave correction at all timeframes
▼
Markets move in 5-wave impulse sequences (waves 1–5, where 1/3/5 move in trend direction and 2/4 are corrections) followed by a 3-wave corrective sequence (ABC). The pattern is fractal — each wave subdivides into the same pattern on smaller timeframes. Wave 3 is almost always the strongest and longest impulse wave. Wave 4 almost never overlaps with Wave 1's price territory (key rule for validation). Most useful for framing probabilistic scenarios, not as a precise mechanical signal — interpretation requires experience.
Key rules
Wave 2 never retraces >100% of Wave 1
Wave 3 is never the shortest impulse wave
Wave 4 never enters Wave 1's territory
Wave 3 typically extends to 161.8% of Wave 1
ABC correction often ends at 61.8% of prior impulse
Strategy use
Best used to identify Wave 3 entries (strongest move) and Wave 5 exhaustion exits. Combine with RSI divergence — Wave 5 with RSI divergence = high-conviction reversal setup. Do not trade mechanically on wave counts alone.
03
Multi-Timeframe Analysis (MTF) Structure
Top-down approach — higher timeframe sets the context, lower timeframe gives the entry
▼
Always analyze from the higher timeframe down. The weekly chart determines the primary trend. The daily chart gives the trade setup. The 4H or 1H gives the precise entry timing. A buy signal on the daily that aligns with a weekly uptrend and monthly support carries far more weight than an isolated daily signal against a weekly downtrend. Most beginner traders lose money because they find a setup on a 5-minute chart that is fighting a daily downtrend.
Timeframe hierarchy
Monthly → Macro trend direction
Weekly → Primary trend + major S/R
Daily → Setup identification
4H / 1H → Entry timing refinement
15m / 5m → Precision entry (active traders)
Confluence scoring rule
A signal aligned on 3+ timeframes is a "high-confluence" trade. Assign a score: weekly trend aligned (+2), daily setup (+2), entry timeframe signal (+1), Fibonacci level (+1), volume confirmation (+1). Score ≥5 = high-conviction trade. Score ≤3 = pass.

These are the critical inter-indicator relationships. Understanding which indicators are redundant vs. complementary prevents over-engineering your system with correlated signals that give a false sense of confirmation.

1. Redundant pairs — avoid using both simultaneously
These pairs measure essentially the same thing. Using both doesn't add confirmation — it adds false confidence.
Indicator A Indicator B Relationship Which to keep
RSI Stochastic Highly correlated Both measure overbought/oversold via price range RSI for trending markets. Stochastic for ranging markets. Never both as "confirmation."
OBV A/D Line Highly correlated Both are cumulative volume flow indicators A/D is more nuanced (uses close position within range). Use A/D as primary, OBV as secondary check.
SMA(200) EMA(200) Highly correlated Both track long-term trend level Use one consistently. SMA for structure reference; EMA for dynamic entries. Don't use both as separate signals.
Stochastic Williams %R Mathematically near-identical Choose one. Williams %R is slightly faster. Stochastic more popular. No added value in using both.
Bollinger Bands Standard Deviation BB is directly derived from StdDev Use BB — it visualizes the same information more clearly. Standard deviation alone adds nothing BB doesn't show.
2. Complementary pairs — genuine independent signals
These pairs measure genuinely different dimensions. Alignment between them is true confluence.
Indicator A Indicator B Why they're complementary Combined signal
RSI (momentum) OBV (volume flow) RSI measures price momentum speed. OBV measures institutional money flow. Independent inputs. Both bullish: strong buy — momentum AND institutional buying aligned. Diverging: institutions not supporting the price move — warning.
MACD (trend momentum) RSI (oscillator) MACD is trend-sensitive and lagging. RSI is faster and mean-reverting. Different speeds, different math. MACD on zero-line + RSI turning from oversold = high-conviction entry. MACD divergence confirmed by RSI divergence = strong reversal signal.
ADX (trend strength) Stochastic (overbought/sold) ADX tells you IF trend-following works. Stochastic works in ranging markets. They are regime selectors for each other. ADX >25: ignore Stochastic overbought/oversold, use trend tools. ADX <20: ignore trend signals, use Stochastic for range reversions.
Fibonacci (structure) RSI (momentum) Fibonacci identifies the price zone. RSI identifies the momentum condition at that zone. Price + timing combination. Price at 61.8% Fib retracement + RSI at 30–40 in an uptrend = one of the highest-probability long entries in all of TA.
Volume Profile (institutional levels) Price Action (candlestick) VP shows where institutional positions exist. PA shows how price behaves when it reaches those levels. Bullish engulfing candle at a High Volume Node = institutional buyers defending their position. Very strong signal.
Bollinger Bands (volatility) MACD (momentum) BB measures price relative to statistical mean. MACD measures momentum direction. Price at lower BB + MACD histogram turning positive = volatility extreme with momentum confirmation. High-probability mean reversion.
ATR (volatility measure) ADX (trend strength) ATR measures how much price moves. ADX measures whether it's directional. Both needed for full volatility picture. High ADX + rising ATR = trending AND expanding — strong trend in progress. High ATR + low ADX = volatile but directionless — dangerous for trend strategies.
3. Price vs. indicator divergences — the highest-conviction signals
When price and indicator move in opposite directions, the indicator often leads price. These divergences are the most powerful signals in TA.
Price action Indicator Divergence type Implication & strategy
Price: Lower Low RSI: Higher Low Bullish divergence Selling pressure is weakening — fewer sellers willing to push price lower. Most reliable on weekly charts. Enter long on confirmation candle. Strongest if RSI was below 30 at the divergence low.
Price: Higher High RSI: Lower High Bearish divergence Buying pressure weakening — fewer buyers willing to push price higher. Enter short/reduce longs on confirmation. Strongest if RSI was above 70 at the divergence high.
Price: Higher High OBV: Lower High Volume bearish divergence Institutions are distributing into the rally — not buying the new high. Higher conviction than RSI divergence alone. Exit or reduce long exposure aggressively.
Price: Lower Low MACD Histogram: Higher Low MACD bullish divergence Momentum of the downtrend is fading. Combine with price near Fibonacci support for maximum conviction. Earlier signal than price action alone.
Price: Higher High MFI: Lower High Volume+momentum bearish divergence Both price momentum AND volume flow diverging — the most serious distribution warning. If all three (RSI + OBV + MFI) diverge simultaneously at a price high, it is an extremely high-conviction exit signal.
4. Regime-dependent correlations — which indicators work in which market type
The same indicator can be highly reliable in one regime and generate nothing but noise in another. ADX is the gate-keeper.
Market regime Works well Avoid Primary identifier
Strong Uptrend ADX >30, price > all MAs Moving averages, Parabolic SAR, MACD zero-line, RS ratio, Ichimoku RSI overbought signals (RSI stays >70 — don't sell), Stochastic (continuous overbought) ADX >30 + price > 50MA > 200MA + OBV rising
Ranging / Choppy ADX <20 RSI (30/70 levels), Stochastic, Bollinger Band mean reversion, Williams %R, S/R bounces MACD crossovers (constant whipsaws), Moving average crosses (noise), Parabolic SAR ADX <20 + price oscillating between defined S/R levels
Strong Downtrend ADX >30, price < all MAs Bearish MAs alignment, MACD bearish zero-line, Parabolic SAR (short), RS ratio declining RSI oversold signals (RSI stays <30 — don't buy dips), Stochastic oversold (persistent) ADX >30 + price < 50MA < 200MA + OBV falling
Volatility Expansion ATR spike, VIX >25 ATR for stop sizing, VIX for sentiment extreme, Volume spikes for climax detection All trend indicators (whipsaws in fast markets), Tight stop losses (get stopped out on noise) ATR rising sharply + VIX spiking + volume 3× average
Volatility Compression BB Squeeze Bollinger/Keltner squeeze setup, Volume dry-up, Decreasing ATR Oscillators (nothing to oscillate), Trend indicators (no trend to follow) BB inside Keltner + volume below 30-day average + ATR at lows

Each timeframe serves a different purpose. The strategy you're building (monthly rebalanced long-only rotation) is primarily a daily/weekly chart system. Here is a complete guide to what each timeframe is for, which indicators work best on it, and how they link together.

Timeframe hierarchy
Monthly chart
Purpose: Macro trend direction. Structural support/resistance that takes months or years to reach.

Best indicators: 200MA, major Fibonacci levels, long-term S/R zones, Elliott Wave major count.

Signal weight: Highest. Monthly S/R overrides all other timeframe signals.

How often to check: Monthly review only.
Weekly chart
Purpose: Primary trend identification. Setup quality assessment. Key S/R for swing trades.

Best indicators: RSI divergences (highest reliability here), MACD, Ichimoku, 50MA, volume trend, RS ratio.

Signal weight: Very high. Weekly RSI divergences are the most reliable reversal signals in TA.

How often to check: Weekly review (your primary analysis session).
Daily chart
Purpose: Trade setup identification and scoring. Entry/exit zone definition.

Best indicators: Candlestick patterns, Chart patterns, 20/50MA, Bollinger Bands, ATR, Volume, OBV, Pivot Points, Fibonacci retracements.

Signal weight: High. Primary setup timeframe for your strategy.

How often to check: Daily (after market close).
4-Hour chart
Purpose: Entry timing refinement. Better price for entries identified on daily chart.

Best indicators: MACD, RSI, Stochastic, VWAP, smaller Fibonacci retracements within daily setup.

Signal weight: Medium. Used to refine entry point, not to identify the trade.

How often to check: When entering a position identified on daily chart.
