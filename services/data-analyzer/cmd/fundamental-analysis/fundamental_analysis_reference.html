Trading System · Module 1

Fundamental Analysis

Complete reference · Quantitative & Qualitative · Data sources · Strategy ranges · Correlations
Tier 1 — Highest impact
01
Earnings Per Share (EPS) & EPS Growth Rate Growth
Net profit attributable to each share — the most watched earnings metric
▼
EPS is the single most watched earnings metric. Markets price expectations — so the direction and rate of EPS revision matters as much as the level itself. Year-over-year EPS growth above 15% is considered strong for most sectors. Earnings surprise (actual vs. analyst consensus) drives the largest single-session price moves of any scheduled event. Always compare trailing EPS to forward EPS estimates to understand momentum direction.
EPS = (Net Income − Preferred Dividends) ÷ Weighted Average Shares Outstanding
Strategy ranges
Strong: YoY growth >15%
Neutral: 5–15% growth
Weak: <5% or declining
Data sources
SEC EDGAR (10-K/10-Q free) · Simfin API · Yahoo Finance (yfinance Python lib) · Macrotrends.net
Watch for
Share buybacks artificially inflate EPS by reducing share count without improving underlying profit. Always cross-check EPS growth with Free Cash Flow growth — if they diverge sharply, the earnings quality is suspect. Also check dilution from stock-based compensation, especially in tech companies.
02
Revenue & Revenue Growth Rate Growth
Total income before expenses — top-line health and market share indicator
▼
Revenue growth reveals whether a company is taking market share or losing it. Revenue quality matters enormously — recurring subscription revenue (SaaS, insurance) is far more valuable than lumpy one-time project revenue. Organic growth (excluding acquisitions) is the purest signal of business momentum. Declining revenue with improving margins is a warning sign: the company is shrinking profitably, which is not sustainable.
Strategy ranges
Strong: >10% YoY (growth co.), >5% (mature)
Neutral: 2–10%
Weak: <2% or negative
Data sources
SEC EDGAR · Simfin · Stockanalysis.com · Macrotrends
03
Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E) Valuation
What the market pays for each dollar of earnings
▼
The most universally used valuation multiple. Always compare P/E to: (1) the sector average, (2) the stock's own 5-year historical mean, and (3) the forward P/E using next year's EPS estimates. A high P/E means growth expectations are fully priced in — any disappointment triggers disproportionate drops. Cyclical companies look expensive at cycle troughs (low earnings) and cheap at peaks — use normalized earnings for these.
Trailing P/E = Price ÷ TTM EPS  |  Forward P/E = Price ÷ Next 12M EPS estimate
Strategy ranges (S&P 500 context)
Value stocks: P/E <15 · Growth stocks: <25
Value: 15–20 · Growth: 25–40
Value: >20 · Growth: >40 (needs justification)
Data sources
Finviz screener · Yahoo Finance · Macrotrends · Simfin API
04
Free Cash Flow (FCF) & FCF Yield Cash Flow
Real cash generated after capex — the hardest earnings metric to manipulate
▼
FCF is what actually funds dividends, buybacks, debt repayment, and acquisitions. Unlike net income, it cannot easily be manipulated through accounting choices. FCF Yield puts it in market price context. Divergence between rising reported earnings and falling FCF is one of the most important red flags in fundamental analysis — always check this cross-reference.
FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures  |  FCF Yield = FCF ÷ Market Cap × 100
Strategy ranges
FCF Yield >5%: attractive
FCF Yield 2–5%: fair
FCF Yield <2% or negative: avoid
Data sources
SEC EDGAR (Cash Flow Statement) · Simfin · Macrotrends · Yahoo Finance
05
Profit Margins (Gross / Operating / Net) Profitability
What percentage of revenue survives at each stage of the income statement
▼
Gross Margin shows pricing power and production efficiency. Operating Margin adds overhead and SG&A — reveals operational efficiency. Net Margin is the final bottom line. The trend direction over 8 consecutive quarters is more important than any single snapshot. Gross margin expansion is typically the earliest signal of improving competitive position. Net margin collapsing while revenue grows = cost structure is broken.
Gross Margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue  |  Operating Margin = Operating Income ÷ Revenue  |  Net Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue
Strategy ranges (sector-dependent)
Gross >40%, Net >15%: strong moat
Gross 20–40%, Net 5–15%: average
Gross <20%, Net <5%: margin pressure
Data sources
SEC EDGAR · Simfin · Macrotrends · Wisesheets
Tier 2 — High importance
06
ROIC & ROE (Return on Invested Capital / Equity) Profitability
How efficiently does the company generate profit from capital?
▼
ROIC is the master profitability metric. ROIC above WACC (typically 8–10%) means the company creates value with every dollar invested. ROE measures returns on shareholders' equity — but can be inflated by taking on debt, so always check alongside D/E ratio. Sustained ROIC >15% for 5+ consecutive years is the classic Buffett-style quality signal indicating a durable competitive moat.
ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital  |  ROE = Net Income ÷ Shareholders' Equity
Strategy ranges
ROIC >15%: excellent / moat indicator
ROIC 8–15%: adequate
ROIC <8%: destroying capital value
Data sources
Simfin · Macrotrends · TIKR.com · Manual calc from SEC EDGAR
07
Debt-to-Equity (D/E) & Net Debt / EBITDA Balance Sheet
Leverage — how much debt finances the business vs. equity
▼
Leverage amplifies returns in good times and losses in bad times. Net Debt/EBITDA is the cleaner metric as it accounts for cash holdings. Rising interest rates make high-debt companies immediately more vulnerable — each refinancing cycle hits earnings harder. D/E above 2× demands scrutiny of debt maturity schedules and interest coverage ratio. Industry context essential: utilities routinely operate at D/E of 3–4× safely.
D/E = Total Debt ÷ Shareholders' Equity  |  Net Debt/EBITDA = (Total Debt − Cash) ÷ EBITDA
Strategy ranges
Net Debt/EBITDA <2×: conservative
2–4×: manageable, monitor
>4×: high risk in rate rise environment
Data sources
SEC EDGAR · Simfin · Finviz · Yahoo Finance
08
EV / EBITDA Valuation
Capital-structure neutral valuation — better than P/E for comparing companies
▼
EV (Enterprise Value) = Market Cap + Debt − Cash. Dividing by EBITDA removes the effect of different capital structures, tax rates, and accounting depreciation choices, making it ideal for cross-company comparison and M&A analysis. Industry medians vary widely: tech 20–30×, industrials 10–15×, utilities 8–12×. Always compare within sector, never cross-sector.
EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash  |  EV/EBITDA = EV ÷ (Operating Income + D&A)
Strategy ranges (broad market)
<10×: value territory
10–20×: fairly valued
>20×: requires strong growth justification
Data sources
Finviz · Macrotrends · TIKR · Calc from Simfin data
09
PEG Ratio Valuation
P/E adjusted for expected earnings growth rate
▼
PEG adjusts P/E for the company's expected growth, making expensive-looking companies fairly valued or cheap once growth is factored in. A P/E of 30 looks expensive until earnings are growing at 40% — PEG of 0.75 is actually attractive. Highly sensitive to the growth rate estimate used — always use 3–5 year analyst consensus, not single-year estimates. Most useful for growth companies where pure P/E misleads.
PEG = P/E Ratio ÷ EPS Annual Growth Rate (%)
Strategy ranges
PEG <1.0: undervalued relative to growth
1.0–2.0: fairly valued
>2.0: growth expectations fully priced
Data sources
Yahoo Finance (statistics tab) · Finviz · Macrotrends
10
Current Ratio & Quick Ratio Balance Sheet
Short-term liquidity — can the company meet near-term obligations?
▼
Current Ratio includes all current assets including inventory. Quick Ratio excludes inventory (which may not be quickly convertible to cash), making it a stricter solvency test. Below 1.0 means short-term liabilities exceed liquid short-term assets — not always fatal but demands explanation. Critical to check when a company has significant debt maturities approaching or operates in a cyclical industry.
Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities  |  Quick Ratio = (Cash + Receivables) ÷ Current Liabilities
Strategy ranges
Current >1.5 · Quick >1.0: safe
Current 1.0–1.5 · Quick 0.7–1.0: monitor
Current <1.0 · Quick <0.7: liquidity risk
Data sources
SEC EDGAR · Simfin · Stockanalysis.com
Tier 3 — Important context
11
Price-to-Book (P/B) & Tangible Book Value Valuation
Market price vs. net asset value on the balance sheet
▼
Most relevant for banks, insurers, and asset-heavy industries. P/B below 1.0 means the market values the company at less than its book value — signals deep value or structural problems. Tangible Book Value strips out intangibles (goodwill, patents) for a harder measure of real asset value. Technology companies with heavy intangible assets make P/B less meaningful — use EV/EBITDA or P/FCF instead.
P/B = Share Price ÷ Book Value Per Share  |  Book Value = Total Assets − Total Liabilities
Strategy ranges
P/B <1.5 (asset-heavy sectors): value signal
1.5–3.0: fair for most industries
>5×: limited margin of safety
Data sources
Finviz · Macrotrends · Simfin API
12
Dividend Yield & Payout Ratio Cash Flow
Income return on investment and dividend sustainability
▼
Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend ÷ Share Price. Payout Ratio = Dividends ÷ Net Income (or FCF for better accuracy). A high yield with a low payout ratio is the ideal — it signals generous income that is sustainable. Payout above 80% leaves little room for dividend maintenance during earnings dips. Dividend cuts are one of the most negative catalysts a stock can experience — the market punishes them severely.
Dividend Yield = (Annual DPS ÷ Price) × 100  |  Payout Ratio = DPS ÷ EPS × 100
Strategy ranges
Yield 3–6%, Payout <60%: sustainable income
Yield >6% — verify payout ratio sustainability
Payout >80%: dividend cut risk
Data sources
Yahoo Finance · Seeking Alpha · Macrotrends
13
Share Count Trend (Buybacks vs. Dilution) Growth
Is the company shrinking or growing its share count over time?
▼
Net buybacks reduce share count, boosting EPS per share without improving underlying earnings — a form of financial engineering. Heavy stock-based compensation (SBC) in tech companies dilutes shareholders even when the company appears profitable. Track the 5-year change in shares outstanding. Declining share count is generally bullish; rising share count demands scrutiny of why capital is being raised.
Strategy ranges
Share count declining >2% annually: buyback program
Flat ±2%: neutral
Growing >3% annually: dilution risk
Data sources
SEC EDGAR · Macrotrends · Simfin
14
Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Intrinsic Value Valuation
The theoretical anchor — what a business is worth based on future cash flows
▼
DCF calculates the present value of all future free cash flows, discounted at the company's cost of capital (WACC). The most theoretically correct valuation method. Extremely sensitive to input assumptions — a 1% change in growth rate or discount rate can move the output by 30–50%. Use DCF as a range and sanity check, not a precise number. Best used alongside multiples-based valuation.
DCF Value = Σ [FCFt ÷ (1 + WACC)^t] + Terminal Value  |  WACC typically 8–12%
Strategy use
Price <70% of DCF intrinsic: strong margin of safety
Price 70–100% of DCF: fairly valued
Price >110% of DCF: downside risk
Data sources
Build from Simfin FCF data + FRED risk-free rate · valueinvesting.io (calculator) · Finbox.com
15
Interest Coverage Ratio Profitability
Can the company comfortably pay the interest on its debt?
▼
Measures how many times over the company can pay its interest expense from operating earnings. Critical for any company carrying significant debt. In a rising rate environment, refinancing at higher rates can quickly erode coverage. Below 2× means interest payments consume more than half of operating earnings — very dangerous in an economic slowdown.
Interest Coverage = EBIT ÷ Interest Expense
Strategy ranges
>5×: very safe
2–5×: adequate, monitor debt maturity
<2×: high risk, especially in rising rate env.
Data sources
Simfin · Macrotrends · Manual calc from SEC EDGAR income statement
16
Asset Turnover & Inventory Turnover Profitability
Operational efficiency — how productively does the company use its assets?
▼
Asset Turnover measures revenue generated per dollar of assets — high turnover means the company extracts maximum output from its asset base. Inventory Turnover reveals how quickly goods move — slowing turnover signals weakening demand or poor inventory management, often before it appears in revenue numbers. Both are most useful for comparing companies within the same industry.
Asset Turnover = Revenue ÷ Average Total Assets  |  Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory
Strategy use
Compare to sector peers over time. Declining inventory turnover is an early warning for consumer and industrial companies. Rising asset turnover indicates improving capital efficiency.
Data sources
Simfin · Stockanalysis.com · Macrotrends
17
Analyst Consensus Estimates & Revision Trend Growth
Where professional analysts expect the business to go — and whether they're upgrading or downgrading
▼
Consensus EPS and revenue estimates set the bar the market prices in. The revision trend (are estimates rising or falling over the past 90 days?) is often a stronger signal than the estimate itself. Rising estimates attract institutional buying before the actual earnings event. The ratio of upward to downward revisions is tracked as the "Earnings Revision Ratio" — above 1.0 is bullish, below is bearish.
Data sources
Yahoo Finance (analysis tab) · Seeking Alpha · Finviz · WSJ Markets
Strategy use
Upward revision trend + improving fundamentals = high-conviction buy signal. Downward revisions + deteriorating margins = exit or avoid signal. Track 30, 60, 90-day revision trend.
18
Goodwill & Intangible Assets as % of Total Assets Balance Sheet
Hidden balance sheet risk from past acquisitions
▼
Goodwill arises when a company acquires another for more than book value. It sits on the balance sheet until written down (impaired). Serial acquirers can accumulate enormous goodwill that inflates total assets. A goodwill impairment charge is a non-cash but significant negative signal — it means the acquisition did not deliver expected returns. Check goodwill as a % of total assets and track it over time.
Strategy ranges
Goodwill <20% of total assets: low risk
20–40%: monitor acquisition discipline
>40%: significant impairment risk
Data sources
SEC EDGAR 10-K Balance Sheet · Simfin
19
Price-to-Sales (P/S) Ratio Valuation
Valuation when earnings are zero or negative — useful for early-stage growth companies
▼
P/S = Market Cap ÷ Annual Revenue. Relevant for companies not yet profitable or with highly variable earnings. It requires an assumption that margins will eventually normalize to industry levels. P/S reached extreme levels during 2020–2021 tech bubble (20–50× for SaaS) before collapsing in 2022. Most useful for comparing within a sector where margin profiles are similar.
P/S = Market Cap ÷ TTM Revenue  |  EV/Revenue = EV ÷ TTM Revenue (preferred for comparisons)
Strategy ranges (SaaS/tech context)
P/S <5× with >20% revenue growth: reasonable
5–10×: growth premium required
>15×: speculative, very high risk
Data sources
Finviz · Simfin · Stockanalysis.com
20
CapEx as % of Revenue & Reinvestment Rate Cash Flow
How much of revenue must be reinvested to maintain and grow the business?
▼
Low CapEx relative to revenue = asset-light business model = high FCF conversion. Asset-light businesses (software, insurance, consumer brands) can grow with minimal reinvestment — this is a core quality characteristic. Capital-intensive industries (semiconductors, airlines, mining) must constantly reinvest large portions of revenue just to maintain capacity, leaving less for shareholder returns.
CapEx Intensity = CapEx ÷ Revenue × 100  |  FCF Conversion = FCF ÷ Net Income × 100
Strategy ranges
CapEx <5% of revenue: asset-light, high quality
5–15%: moderate capital intensity
>20%: capital-intensive, FCF constrained
Data sources
SEC EDGAR Cash Flow Statement · Simfin · Macrotrends

Qualitative analysis cannot be reduced to a number — it requires reading, judgment, and pattern recognition. The LLM layer in your system handles most of this. Each factor below includes what to look for, how to score it, and where the source documents live.

Tier 1 — Most decisive qualitative factors
🏰
Competitive Moat & Pricing Power Qualitative
Can the business raise prices without losing customers?
▼
The single most important qualitative characteristic. A moat is a sustainable competitive advantage that prevents competitors from eroding profitability. The five types are: network effects (value grows with users), switching costs (expensive or painful to leave), cost advantages (permanently lower cost structure), intangible assets (brands, patents, licenses), and efficient scale (dominant in a market too small for a second player).
What to look for in filings
Sustained gross margins above 40% over 10 years. Ability to raise prices above inflation without volume loss. Customer retention / churn rates (in 10-K MD&A). Revenue per customer trending up. Competitor commentary in annual reports. Barriers to entry description in 10-K Item 1 (Business).
LLM scoring prompt
Ask: "Identify the competitive moat type, cite specific evidence from the filing, and rate moat strength on a scale of 1–5 with justification." Input: 10-K Item 1 + last 4 earnings call transcripts.
Source documents
SEC EDGAR 10-K Item 1 Earnings call transcripts (Seeking Alpha) Annual report letter to shareholders Industry analyst reports (sell-side)
👔
Management Quality & Capital Allocation Qualitative
Are the people running the company trustworthy, capable, and aligned with shareholders?
▼
Outstanding management compounds returns over time; poor management destroys even great businesses. Assess capital allocation history: did acquisitions create or destroy value? Are buybacks done at reasonable prices or just to hit EPS targets? Insider ownership above 10% aligns incentives. Candor in shareholder letters — do they acknowledge mistakes or only celebrate wins?
What to look for
Insider ownership % and purchase/sale history (SEC Form 4). CEO tenure and track record at prior companies. Compensation vs. performance alignment (proxy statement). History of M&A returns (did past acquisitions generate ROIC > WACC?). Guidance accuracy history.
Data sources
SEC Form 4 (insider transactions) DEF 14A Proxy Statement Glassdoor CEO ratings Earnings call transcripts Annual shareholder letters
📈
Earnings Call Tone & Management Narrative Qualitative
What management says, how they say it, and what they avoid saying
▼
The LLM layer adds enormous value here. Management language changes before numbers change. Increasing use of vague language, more lawyer-drafted answers, defensive tone about a previously celebrated product, sudden emphasis on "efficiency" when they previously emphasized growth — all precede negative surprises. Conversely, increasing specificity about customers, pipeline, and metrics signals genuine confidence.
LLM scoring signals
Sentiment polarity vs. prior 4 quarters. Frequency of hedging language. Specificity of forward guidance. Changes in language around key business metrics. Q&A tone vs. scripted section tone. Length of answers to challenging analyst questions.
Data sources
Seeking Alpha transcripts (free) The Motley Fool transcripts Company IR page (audio/webcast) Rev.com (real-time transcription)
⚠️
10-K Risk Factors & MD&A Analysis Qualitative
What the company itself discloses as its own biggest threats
▼
Item 1A (Risk Factors) in the 10-K is legally required to be complete and material. Most investors skip it. The LLM should compare this year's risk factors to last year's — new risks added, risks removed, and changes in emphasis are meaningful signals. The MD&A (Management Discussion & Analysis) is where management explains the numbers — changes in language here often precede financial statement changes.
LLM tasks
Year-over-year diff of risk factor section. Flag newly added material risks. Score language change in MD&A — is management more or less confident than prior year? Identify any litigation risks and their potential financial materiality.
Data sources
SEC EDGAR 10-K (Item 1A, Item 7) SEC EDGAR 10-Q (quarterly updates) SEC 8-K (material event disclosures)
Tier 2 — Important qualitative context
🏭
Industry Structure & Competitive Dynamics Qualitative
Porter's Five Forces — is the industry structure inherently profitable?
▼
Even the best company struggles in a structurally bad industry. Assess: threat of new entrants (capital requirements, regulatory barriers), supplier bargaining power, customer bargaining power, threat of substitutes, and rivalry intensity. Airlines have chronically bad industry structure despite serving billions of customers. Software has structurally excellent economics. Industry structure persists for decades.
Data sources
IBISWorld industry reports 10-K Item 1 (competition section) Sell-side sector initiation reports Trade association publications
LLM task
Score each of the Five Forces on a 1–5 scale using 10-K and industry sources. Output a structured JSON with force scores and evidence citations.
🔬
R&D Pipeline & Innovation Trajectory Qualitative
Is the company investing in its future or harvesting its past?
▼
R&D as a % of revenue signals reinvestment in future products. Critically, check whether R&D is producing results — are new products gaining traction? Is the company losing ground to newer entrants? Patent filing trends, product pipeline descriptions in 10-K, and management commentary about upcoming product cycles all feed this assessment. Most relevant for pharma, tech, semiconductor, and industrial companies.
Data sources
10-K (R&D expense + pipeline description) USPTO patent database FDA pipeline tracker (pharma) Earnings call product discussion
Strategy range
Tech: R&D 10–20% of revenue = healthy. Pharma: 15–25%. Under-investment in R&D relative to sector peers signals a company harvesting its position rather than building it.
⚖️
Regulatory Environment & Legal Risk Qualitative
Pending litigation, regulatory changes, and compliance burdens
▼
Regulatory risk is underpriced until it is suddenly very expensive. Antitrust actions against big tech, environmental regulations for energy companies, pricing regulations for pharma — these can materially alter business models with limited warning. Track DOJ/FTC filings, EU regulatory actions, and congressional hearing schedules for specific sectors. SEC enforcement actions are also meaningful signals of governance problems.
Data sources
SEC EDGAR 10-K Item 3 (Legal Proceedings) DOJ Antitrust Division (justice.gov) FTC.gov enforcement actions EU Competition (ec.europa.eu) Reuters/Bloomberg legal feeds
LLM task
Summarize all active legal proceedings from 10-K Item 3. Flag any with potential liability >5% of market cap. Track year-over-year changes in disclosed proceedings.
🌍
ESG Factors & Sustainability Risk Qualitative
Environmental, social and governance risks that affect long-term business viability
▼
ESG is a practical risk assessment, not just ethics. Carbon-intensive businesses face stranded asset risk as regulations tighten. Companies with poor labor practices face wage pressure and reputational damage. Weak governance (concentrated voting, no board independence, related-party transactions) correlates with shareholder value destruction. Institutional ownership screening increasingly excludes poor ESG scores, creating structural selling pressure.
Data sources
MSCI ESG Ratings (msci.com) Sustainalytics (morningstar) CDP climate disclosure database Company sustainability report
What matters most
Governance (G) is the most predictive of financial outcomes. Environmental risk is most material for energy, mining, utilities. Social risk matters most for consumer-facing companies. Use as a risk flag, not a primary signal.
📰
News Sentiment & Media Narrative Qualitative
Real-time qualitative signal from news flow and public perception
▼
News sentiment is a noisy but fast-moving signal. The LLM should score news on a rolling 7-day and 30-day basis, filtering for material events (product launches, management changes, regulatory filings, analyst upgrades/downgrades) vs. noise. Sudden shift from positive to negative narrative often precedes price weakness by 5–10 trading days. The absence of negative news during a price decline can indicate institutional selling rather than fundamental problems.
Data sources
NewsAPI.org (free tier available) Alpha Vantage News API Benzinga News API Google News RSS feeds Reddit API (WSB/stocks) StockTwits API
LLM output format
{"sentiment_7d": 0.6, "sentiment_30d": 0.3, "trend": "deteriorating", "material_events": ["CEO departure announced"], "noise_ratio": "high"}
🏦
Insider Transactions & Institutional Ownership Changes Qualitative
What are the people with the most information actually doing with their money?
▼
Cluster buying by multiple insiders (3+ in 90 days) is one of the highest-conviction bullish signals available. Institutional 13F filings (quarterly) reveal hedge fund and mutual fund positioning. When a high-quality fund initiates or dramatically increases a position, the fundamental case is worth examining closely. Insider selling is less informative than buying — insiders sell for many reasons, but buy for only one.
Data sources
SEC Form 4 (EDGAR insider filings) SEC 13F filings (quarterly) Openinsider.com (free aggregator) Dataroma.com (guru holdings) Finviz insider transactions tab
Signal thresholds
Bullish: 3+ insiders buying within 90 days · Large fund (Ackman, Einhorn etc.) initiating position · Institutional ownership rising for 2+ consecutive quarters · CEO buying open-market shares

These are the critical relationships between quantitative FA metrics. Understanding how they move together — and when they diverge — is more valuable than any single metric in isolation. Divergences between correlated metrics are the most powerful signals.

1. Earnings quality cluster
EPS, FCF, Revenue, and Net Margin should all trend in the same direction. When they diverge, something is wrong.
Metric A Metric B Expected relationship What divergence means
EPS Growth FCF Growth Positive Both should rise together EPS rising, FCF flat/falling = earnings manipulation via accruals. Investigate receivables growth and capitalized expenses. This is one of the most important red flags in FA.
Revenue Growth EPS Growth Positive EPS grows faster in operating leverage Revenue falling, EPS rising = buybacks masking deterioration. Revenue rising, EPS falling = cost structure breaking, margin compression.
Gross Margin Net Margin Positive Gross margin leads net margin Gross margin expanding, net margin falling = SG&A or interest costs exploding. Gross margin is the first line of defense — watch it first.
Revenue Growth Gross Margin Context-dependent Fast revenue growth with falling gross margin = scaling without pricing power. Fast growth with stable/rising gross margin = the ideal quality-growth combination.
2. Valuation vs. quality cluster
Valuation multiples should reflect underlying quality. When they don't, it signals mis-pricing.
Metric A Metric B Expected relationship What divergence means
P/E Ratio EPS Growth Rate Positive Higher growth justifies higher P/E Low P/E + high growth = potential undervaluation (check PEG). High P/E + low/declining growth = valuation trap, avoid.
P/E Ratio ROIC Positive High ROIC companies deserve premium P/E High ROIC + low P/E = rare value opportunity. Low ROIC + high P/E = unjustified premium, dangerous combination.
EV/EBITDA Net Debt/EBITDA Inverse Higher debt should compress EV multiple Highly leveraged companies with high EV/EBITDA are doubly exposed — overvalued AND fragile. In rate rise environments this is a short candidate.
FCF Yield Dividend Yield Positive FCF yield should exceed dividend yield Dividend yield > FCF yield = dividend is not covered by real cash generation. Cut risk is high. This divergence is a reliable dividend cut predictor.
P/B Ratio ROE Positive High ROE justifies premium to book Low P/B + high sustained ROE = classic deep value. High P/B + declining ROE = multiple contraction risk ahead.
3. Leverage & liquidity cluster
Balance sheet metrics that interact to determine financial resilience under stress.
Metric A Metric B Expected relationship What divergence means
Net Debt/EBITDA Interest Coverage Inverse High leverage = lower coverage High debt/EBITDA + falling interest coverage = accelerating financial distress. The combination that precedes most corporate credit events.
Current Ratio FCF Trend Positive Strong FCF supports liquidity Deteriorating current ratio + negative FCF = cash burn accelerating. Company may need to raise capital, diluting equity or issuing expensive debt.
D/E Ratio Net Margin Context-dependent Leverage is safe only with strong margins. D/E >3 + net margin <5% = dangerous combination — any revenue shortfall cascades to insolvency risk.
Goodwill % Assets FCF Conversion Often inverse Serial acquirers with high goodwill often show strong reported earnings but weak FCF — the acquisitions are not generating real cash. Watch for widening gap between net income and FCF in acquisition-heavy companies.
4. Operational efficiency cluster
How well the business converts inputs to profitable outputs.
Metric A Metric B Expected relationship What divergence means
ROIC Revenue Growth Positive High ROIC + growth = compounding machine Both rising = the ideal investment. Revenue growing, ROIC declining = growth is dilutive — company is investing in projects that earn less than cost of capital.
Asset Turnover Net Margin DuPont tradeoff The DuPont equation: ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Leverage. High turnover / low margin (Walmart) vs. low turnover / high margin (luxury goods) are both valid models. When both decline together, ROE collapses.
Inventory Turnover Gross Margin Often positive Falling inventory turnover + falling gross margin = demand destruction. Products not selling → price cuts to clear inventory → margin compression. Classic early recession signal for consumer/industrial companies.
CapEx % Revenue FCF Yield Inverse High capex consumes FCF Companies with falling CapEx intensity (transitioning from growth to harvest phase) see FCF yields expand dramatically — this often signals an upcoming re-rating by the market.
5. The master divergence signals — highest conviction
These specific divergence combinations have the highest historical predictive value for either opportunity or risk.
Signal type Metrics involved Interpretation
★ Bullish divergence Low P/E + High ROIC + Rising FCF + Falling D/E + Insider buying All five pointing the same direction is a rare convergence. A quality business becoming cheaper while improving fundamentals and insiders buying = highest conviction long entry.
★ Hidden value signal EPS stagnant + FCF rising strongly + Low P/FCF Earnings held down by non-cash charges (depreciation of past capex) while real cash generation is strong. The market prices on EPS; you buy on FCF. Classic Graham-style hidden value.
★ Deterioration warning EPS rising + FCF flat/falling + Receivables growing faster than revenue Earnings are being manufactured through accrual accounting — customers are buying but not paying, or revenue is being recognized before cash is collected. Restatement or earnings miss risk is elevated.
★ Value trap signal Low P/E + declining ROIC + rising D/E + declining revenue Cheap for a reason. The business is structurally deteriorating. Value investors attracted by the low P/E get trapped as earnings keep declining and the multiple stays low or compresses further.
★ Leverage cycle warning Net Debt/EBITDA rising + Interest Coverage falling + FCF declining + Current Ratio below 1 Four leverage/liquidity metrics simultaneously deteriorating = financial distress trajectory. In a rising rate environment this combination can move to credit event within 2–4 quarters.
